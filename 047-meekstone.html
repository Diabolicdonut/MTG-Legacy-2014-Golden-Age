Entry 047 · The Foundational Canon

The Giants Stayed Down

Meekstone made power three the line between creatures that recovered and creatures that remained trapped, rewarding vigilance, small attackers, temporary pump, and every deck prepared to break a symmetrical rule.

I · The Weight

The Giants Stayed Down

Meekstone does nothing to a creature merely because it is large. The giant may enter, attack, block, activate abilities, and dominate the battlefield. The punishment begins only after it becomes tapped. Then the next untap step arrives, and strength itself becomes the reason it cannot rise.

For one generic mana, the artifact places a permanent rule around every creature with power three or greater. It does not destroy them, exile them, or prevent their abilities. It creates a battlefield in which small creatures recover normally and large creatures are forced to treat every tap as a commitment.

That asymmetry made Meekstone both a prison card and an aggressive weapon. Control decks used Icy Manipulator to push large threats into the trap. Weenie decks attacked beneath the threshold. Vigilant creatures ignored it. Pump spells allowed a small attacker to become enormous only after it had already attacked—and to shrink again before the next untap.

II · The Scale

Alpha’s one-mana equalizer

Alpha’s creatures were frequently expensive, and many of its most visually impressive threats had power three or greater. Serra Angel, Sengir Vampire, Mahamoti Djinn, Force of Nature, and Lord of the Pit all crossed the line.

Meekstone gave any deck a one-mana answer that did not care about color, regeneration, toughness, or protection from a particular hue. It could restrain creatures that black removal could not target or threats too large for red damage.

The tradeoff was fundamental. The artifact did not stop the first attack and did not affect an untapped creature at all. Its efficiency existed because it required time, tapping, and a deck capable of exploiting the delay.

Meekstone did not make the weak stronger. It made strength expensive to use twice.Power converted into an untap restriction
III · The Number

Why three power is the line

Two-power creatures untap normally. Three-power creatures do not. That single point divides the battlefield into creatures that can participate every turn and creatures that must find another method of recovery.

Power 0–2

Below the stone

The creature untaps normally, even if its toughness, mana value, or other abilities are enormous.

Power 3+

Held down

If tapped when the untap step arrives, the creature remains tapped.

Variable power

The threshold can move

Counters, Auras, Equipment, static effects, and temporary modifications can determine which side of the line a creature occupies.

The choice of three was especially meaningful in early Magic. Many efficient small creatures occupied the one- and two-power range, while signature finishers began at three or four. Meekstone did not stop creature decks generally. It separated armies from monsters.

IV · The Restriction

What Meekstone actually does

Meekstone creates a static restriction during each player’s untap step. A creature with power three or greater cannot untap as part of that untap step.

The artifact does not tap creatures. It does not stop a creature from attacking, blocking, using a tap ability, being targeted by an untap spell, or untapping outside the untap step. It also does not prevent an already untapped creature from remaining untapped.

Multiple Meekstones are redundant. One copy and four copies impose the same restriction; additional copies provide insurance against removal rather than increasing the threshold or effect.

Restriction, not a trigger. Meekstone does not place an ability on the stack during the untap step. The affected creature simply fails to untap, and players do not receive priority during that step to change its power at the last moment.
Quinton Hoover’s original MeekstoneThe small engraved object glows against a dark background while two enormous hands reach toward it. The stone’s scale is the visual joke: the larger the claimant, the more completely it dominates them.
V · The Measurement

Power is checked during untapping

A creature’s current power determines whether it may untap. A normally small creature enlarged permanently by counters or an Aura can become trapped. A normally large creature reduced below three before the untap step can recover.

Because no player receives priority during the untap step, a temporary change must already be in place before that step begins. An instant cannot be cast midway through untapping to shrink a creature and rescue it.

Effects that last “until end of turn” normally expire during the cleanup step, well before the next untap. That timing allows temporary pump spells to work unusually well with Meekstone.

VI · The Commitment

The creature must first become tapped

Meekstone’s greatest limitation is that an untapped creature remains completely functional. A large creature can stay back to block indefinitely, activate nontap abilities, or threaten an attack without entering the trap.

The controller of the creature decides whether attacking is worth the risk of remaining tapped. The Meekstone player must often force the issue through attacks, tap effects, combat tricks, or a race that makes blocking insufficient.

This distinguishes the artifact from Ensnaring Bridge or Moat. Those cards prevent combat directly. Meekstone changes what happens after combat and asks the rest of the deck to create the initial tap.

VII · The Hand

Icy Manipulator closes the trap

Icy Manipulator supplies exactly what Meekstone lacks. It taps the largest creature before combat or during the opponent’s end step. If that creature still has power three or greater when the next untap step begins, Meekstone keeps it down.

The pair became one of early control’s clearest soft locks. Icy could address one new creature each turn while Meekstone preserved the work of every previous activation. The controller’s one-mana artifact converted temporary tapping into long-term suppression.

Creature-removal spells could then be reserved for threats below the threshold, creatures with vigilance, or utility creatures that mattered without attacking.

VIII · The Exception

Vigilance walks around the stone

A creature with vigilance does not tap when it attacks. If nothing else taps it, Meekstone has no opportunity to restrain it.

This interaction was central to Zak Dolan’s 1994 World Championship deck. Serra Angel had four power, but its vigilance allowed it to attack while remaining untapped. The opponent’s large creatures could be trapped by Meekstone and Icy Manipulator while Dolan’s primary finisher continued attacking every turn.

Meekstone therefore does not simply reward small creatures. It rewards any creature designed to function without becoming tapped: vigilance creatures, creatures with untap abilities, and threats that deal damage without combat.

IX · The Army

Small creatures break the symmetry

A deck built from one- and two-power creatures can treat Meekstone as nearly one-sided. Kird Ape normally has two power even when its Forest bonus increases toughness. Mishra's Factory attacks as a 2/2. Llanowar Elves, Scarwood Goblins, and Scavenger Folk remain below the line.

The opponent’s Serra Angels, Erhnam Djinns, Sengir Vampires, and large artifact creatures may attack once and remain tapped. The small deck continues using every creature each turn.

This is a different philosophy from Icy prison. The aggressive deck does not need to control the battlefield forever. It needs the opponent’s best blocker or attacker to miss enough turns for modest creatures to finish the race.

X · The Burst

Temporary power makes the artifact aggressive

Giant Growth, Blood Lust, and Berserk allow a two-power creature to attack as something enormous. The creature becomes tapped during combat while its power is above the Meekstone threshold—but the pump effect usually expires during cleanup.

By the next untap step, the creature has returned to its printed power and untaps normally. The opponent’s naturally large creature remains trapped because its power never falls.

This creates one of Meekstone’s most elegant asymmetries. The aggressive deck borrows size only when size is useful. The opposing deck owns size permanently and pays the permanent cost.

XI · The Champion

Meekstone at the first World Championship

Zak Dolan’s 1994 World Championship deck played two Meekstones inside a dense control-prison structure. Stasis, Winter Orb, Kismet, Icy Manipulator, removal, and unusual creatures all attacked different ways an opponent might develop.

Dolan described his plan as controlling the play environment so completely that even at one life the opponent could do nothing. Meekstone contributed by making large threats poor long-term investments. A creature might attack once, but every later combat required an answer to the artifact or a way to untap outside the normal step.

The card’s inclusion in the first World Champion’s deck established it as more than a kitchen-table annoyance. It was part of competitive Magic’s first public vocabulary of prison.

XII · The Threshold

Old Man of the Sea beneath the threshold

Old Man of the Sea has two power, placing it safely beneath Meekstone. Its tap ability can gain control of a creature whose power does not exceed the Old Man’s power.

That relationship allowed Dolan’s deck to operate with creatures specifically selected for the prison. Old Man could untap normally and manipulate small opposing creatures; Meekstone restrained larger ones; Serra Angel attacked without tapping.

The card choices look eccentric when read separately. Together they form a battlefield divided by power, tapping, and vigilance. Meekstone was the rule that made those divisions strategically coherent.

XIII · The Sideboard

A three-copy sideboard plan at Worlds 2001

Antoine Ruel’s third-place Nether-Go deck at the 2001 World Championship placed three Meekstones in its sideboard. The main deck’s only creature was Nether Spirit, a recurring 2/2 that remained below the threshold.

The sideboard plan allowed a blue-black-red permission deck to answer large creatures with a single one-mana permanent. Countermagic and removal protected the artifact, while Nether Spirit could continue blocking or attacking normally.

This later appearance shows the card’s enduring sideboard logic. A deck need not build completely around Meekstone when its own threats naturally ignore the text and a particular matchup depends on creatures above the line.

XIV · The Scale

Power creep changed the targets

Modern creatures are often larger for their mana cost, making the threshold appear more relevant. At the same time, they increasingly generate value before Meekstone can matter.

A creature may draw a card, create tokens, destroy a permanent, or trigger an ability as it enters. Preventing that creature from untapping after one attack does not reverse the value already produced. Haste also guarantees immediate use before the artifact can restrain future turns.

Power creep therefore cuts both ways. More creatures qualify for the restriction, but fewer competitive creatures depend solely on attacking repeatedly to justify their card slot.

XV · The Difference

Why it is not creature removal

A tapped creature locked by Meekstone remains on the battlefield. Its static abilities function. Its triggered abilities function. It may be sacrificed, equipped, enchanted, used as a cost, returned to hand, or untapped by another effect.

The opponent can also destroy Meekstone and recover every trapped creature during the next untap step. Unlike true removal, the artifact stores problems rather than eliminating them.

The reward is efficiency. One mana can restrain several large creatures simultaneously and ignore indestructibility, protection, and toughness. The cost is fragility and incompleteness: the creatures are still there, waiting for the stone to leave.

XVI · The Table

The multiplayer equalizer

In Commander, Meekstone can affect three opposing battlefields for one mana. Voltron commanders, Dragons, Dinosaurs, Demons, and large token strategies may all find attacking to be a one-turn commitment.

The controller can build beneath the threshold with utility creatures, toughness-based attackers, vigilance, tap effects, or commanders that win without combat. Because the artifact does not specify opponents, the deck must plan around its own creatures as carefully as everyone else’s.

The political effect is visible. Meekstone may protect the entire table from one dominant creature deck, but it may also convince opponents that the artifact’s controller is the obstacle preventing everyone’s commanders from functioning.

XVII · The Bloodline

The descendants of Meekstone

Crackdown applies a related untap restriction to nonwhite creatures with power three or greater. Marble Titan places a power-based untap rule on a creature body and affects creatures with power three or greater.

The modern tempo relative

Blind Obedience

Blind Obedience does not keep creatures tapped after combat, but it makes opposing creatures and artifacts enter tapped. Like Meekstone, it turns time and tapped status into a broad defensive resource rather than destroying individual threats.

Arena of the Ancients taps legendary creatures and prevents them from untapping normally. Storage Matrix restricts which permanent type a player can untap. Ensnaring Bridge prevents large creatures from attacking based on hand size rather than power alone.

Later descendants usually add color, increase the cost, narrow the affected class, or arrive on a more vulnerable card type. Meekstone remains unique because one generic mana establishes a global power threshold.

XVIII · The Image

The stone that humbles strength

Quinton Hoover’s original art depicts a tiny carved stone glowing between enormous hands. The composition makes scale itself the subject: the object looks insignificant, but the beings reaching for it appear unable to master it.

David Martin’s Seventh Edition replacement places the stone within a clearer fantasy environment and emphasizes the physical burden imposed on a large figure. Steve Argyle’s Kaladesh Invention transforms the effect into an elaborate filigreed device worthy of a plane where mechanisms are treated as art.

Mystery Booster 2 returned David Martin’s image in a Future Sight frame, presenting the ancient one-mana prison as though it were a design sent backward from another version of Magic.

XIX · The Habitats

A life across formats

Old School

A prison and aggro equalizer

Control decks combine it with Icy Manipulator, while low-power creature decks use pump spells and small attackers to break the symmetry.

Legacy and Vintage

Legal, highly specialized

The card occasionally appears through artifact tutors or sideboards, but immediate value and cheap removal limit its competitive role.

Commander

A one-mana global restraint

Large multiplayer boards magnify the effect, especially in decks built around vigilance, toughness, tap control, or small utility creatures.

Meekstone is not legal in Standard, Pioneer, Modern, Pauper, or current digital-only formats such as Timeless. Its recognized competitive homes are Legacy, Vintage, Commander, Oathbreaker, Old School variants, and Cube.

XX · The Feeling

What players remember

The creature attacked once. The stone remembered how strong it was.
Meekstone made every large creature’s tap feel permanent until the artifact itself could be answered.

Players remember tapping a Serra Angel with Icy Manipulator and leaving it sideways for turn after turn. They remember discovering that vigilance was not merely an offensive ability, and that a Giant Growth could expire in time to let a small creature untap.

They remember the contradiction in the name. The stone was meek only in size. Everything larger was required to bow to it.

XXII · The Object

Printings and sources

Meekstone carried Quinton Hoover’s original artwork from Alpha through Fifth Edition. Seventh Edition introduced David Martin’s replacement art and the card’s first foil printing. The Kaladesh Inventions series gave it a premium Steve Argyle interpretation in 2017, and Mystery Booster 2 returned David Martin’s image in a Future Sight frame in 2024.
