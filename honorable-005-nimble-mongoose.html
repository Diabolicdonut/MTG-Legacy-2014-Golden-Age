I · The Untargetable

The Goose That Could Not Be Touched

Nimble Mongoose begins as one of the least imposing creatures in Magic: one green mana, one power, one toughness, no evasion, and no ability to accept help from the player who cast it. Then the graveyard reaches seven cards, and the harmless animal becomes a three-power threat that much of the opponent’s deck cannot interact with at all.

Legacy players called it “the Goose.” The nickname is anatomically wrong and strategically perfect. A Mongoose could slip beneath countermagic on the first turn, ignore Swords to Plowshares and Lightning Bolt, then spend several turns looking irrelevant while the deck quietly assembled threshold. Once the seventh card arrived, the clock had already entered the battlefield.

The creature became a bridge between three generations of tempo: Odyssey’s graveyard decks, pre-Delver Canadian Threshold, and the RUG Delver shells of the early 2010s. Across all three, the principle remained unchanged. Cast one cheap threat, spend the rest of the game ensuring that the opponent never receives a clean turn to answer it.

II · The Graveyard

Odyssey’s smallest threshold threat

Odyssey asked players to reconsider card advantage. Discarding cards, milling oneself, sacrificing lands, and spending spells could become progress because the graveyard was no longer merely a record of lost resources. It was an engine that naturally filled as the game continued.

Nimble Mongoose distilled the set’s threshold mechanic into its most efficient body. Before threshold, it was deliberately below rate. After threshold, it became a one-mana 3/3 whose protection from targeting made the original investment extraordinarily difficult to answer.

The design was not self-contained. A deck had to choose cards such as Careful Study, Mental Note, Wild Mongrel, flashback spells, and fetch lands because the Mongoose could not transform on schedule through ordinary play alone.

The Mongoose entered before the graveyard was ready and survived long enough to become the reason it mattered.A one-mana threat paid for with future cards
III · The Threshold

Why the number was seven

Richard Garfield’s threshold mechanic used a resource that naturally increased over time. Early Odyssey design files experimented with ten cards, and development eventually returned the requirement to seven.

One to six

A protected 1/1

The Mongoose can begin attacking and occupying the battlefield, but even small blockers can contain it.

Seven or more

A one-mana 3/3

The transformation requires no activation, additional mana, or trigger. It remains active while the count stays high enough.

Shared condition

The deck turns on together

Werebear, threshold lands, and other Odyssey cards reward the same graveyard work, making the mechanic intentionally linear.

Seven is high enough to require planning but low enough to reach during a competitive game. Every fetch land, cantrip, counterspell, burn spell, and self-mill effect advances the count while performing another necessary job.

IV · The State

What Nimble Mongoose actually does

Shroud means Nimble Mongoose cannot be the target of spells or abilities controlled by any player. A spell that requires a target cannot legally choose it, and an Aura spell cannot target it while being cast.

Threshold is an ability word rather than a keyword ability with independent rules. The printed sentence continuously checks whether the Mongoose’s controller has seven or more cards in their graveyard. At seven or more, it receives +2/+2; below seven, the bonus disappears immediately.

Effects that do not target can still affect it. Sweepers, sacrifice instructions, combat damage, global power changes, and abilities using words such as “each” or “all” work normally.

No transformation trigger. The Mongoose becomes 3/3 the moment the seventh card reaches the graveyard and becomes 1/1 the moment the count falls below seven. Nothing is placed on the stack.
Terese Nielsen’s original Nimble MongooseThe animal is almost lost inside green motion and foliage. Its defense is not armor but the inability of the eye—and eventually the opponent’s removal—to hold it in one place.
V · The Barrier

Protection from both players

Shroud is stronger and more awkward than it first appears. The opponent cannot aim Swords to Plowshares, Lightning Bolt, Fatal Push, or an opposing ability at the Mongoose.

Its controller also cannot use Giant Growth, Equipment’s equip ability, targeted Auras, protection spells, or ordinary targeted pump. The creature refuses assistance as completely as it refuses removal.

Canadian Threshold turned that drawback into deck-building clarity. The deck did not plan to enhance the Mongoose after casting it. It enhanced the conditions around it—graveyard size, opposing mana, combat lanes, and the number of turns available to attack.

VI · The Count

The graveyard becomes a growth counter

Threshold decks treat the graveyard like a counter shared by several cards. Mental Note adds two cards and replaces itself. Careful Study moves three cards toward the graveyard count while selecting which cards remain in hand. Fetch lands become two graveyard cards over the sequence of playing and sacrificing them.

Legacy tempo made the process nearly invisible. Brainstorm and Ponder found action. Daze and Force of Will protected the opening. Stifle and Lightning Bolt disrupted the opponent. Every spell naturally moved toward the graveyard after doing its primary job.

The best threshold enablers were therefore not cards whose only text said “fill your graveyard.” They were useful spells whose normal lifecycle happened to feed the Goose.

VII · The Collapse

Falling below threshold can kill it

The bonus is reversible. If graveyard hate exiles enough cards to reduce the count below seven, Nimble Mongoose immediately returns to 1/1.

This can cause a creature to die without receiving new damage. A threshold Mongoose with two damage marked on it is a damaged 3/3. If the graveyard falls to six cards, it becomes a 1/1 with two damage and is put into the graveyard as a state-based action.

Tormod's Crypt, Relic of Progenitus, Deathrite Shaman, delve, escape, and the controller’s own graveyard costs can therefore function as indirect creature removal.

VIII · The Sky

Le Wonder Goose

Raphaël Lévy’s 2002 World Championship deck was released as “Le Wonder Goose.” Four Nimble Mongooses and four Werebears supplied threshold threats, while Wild Mongrel, Careful Study, Breakthrough, and Mental Note rapidly stocked the graveyard.

Wonder solved the Mongoose’s inability to accept targeted evasion. Once Wonder was in the graveyard and Lévy controlled an Island, every creature gained flying through a global static ability. Shroud did not interfere.

The deck could therefore turn its protected 3/3 into a protected flying 3/3 without ever choosing it as a target. The solution captured Odyssey block at its most coherent: the graveyard enabled the size, protection, and evasion of the same threat.

IX · The Block

Threshold’s undefeated day at Worlds

Dave Humpherys played U/G Threshold during the Odyssey Block Constructed portion of the 2002 World Championship and completed the day undefeated. His list used four Mongooses, four Werebears, Wild Mongrel, Wonder, Mental Note, Careful Study, and Standstill.

Standstill made the cheap protected threat especially punishing. The Mongoose could enter first; Standstill could then discourage the opponent from casting the spell needed to recover while the creature continued attacking.

The result demonstrated that threshold was not merely a Standard package benefiting from a broad card pool. Within Odyssey block itself, the Mongoose was already efficient enough to organize an undefeated tournament deck around it.

X · The Lineage

From Gro to Threshold

Extended “Gro” strategies had already established a tempo formula: play a small number of cheap threats, cast many low-cost or free spells, and use the resulting efficiency to attack while protecting the lead.

Quirion Dryad became larger as spells were cast. Werebear and Nimble Mongoose became larger as those spells reached the graveyard. The threats changed, but the surrounding structure—cantrips, permission, mana efficiency, and an intentionally tiny creature suite—remained recognizable.

Mongoose was ideal for the transition because it did not need to grow one spell at a time on the battlefield. It could arrive after the graveyard was already prepared or wait safely while the rest of the deck crossed the threshold.

XI · The Name

Why the deck became Canadian Threshold

The Legacy archetype known as Canadian Threshold is associated with Canadian players David Caplan and Lam Pham. Caplan piloted the strategy to a Top 8 finish at Gen Con in 2007.

The deck reduced its creature package to four Nimble Mongooses and four Tarmogoyfs. Red supplied Lightning Bolt and Fire // Ice. Blue supplied Brainstorm, cheap counters, and Stifle. Green supplied two threats efficient enough to win almost alone.

“Threshold” remained in the name even as Tarmogoyf replaced Werebear and the deck contained only one threshold card. Nimble Mongoose was sufficient to preserve the identity of the entire archetype.

XII · The Missing Turn

Stifle and Wasteland buy the turns

A one-mana 3/3 is not impressive if the opponent calmly reaches four or five mana and casts sweepers or larger threats. Canadian Threshold prevented that calm development.

Stifle countered fetch-land activations and important triggers. Wasteland destroyed nonbasic lands. Daze punished players who tapped low, while Force of Will stopped the spell that mattered regardless of mana.

The Mongoose did not need to deal twenty damage quickly. It needed every opposing turn to accomplish slightly less than expected. Mana denial converted a modest clock into an inevitable one.

XIII · The Pair

The eight-creature era

Tarmogoyf gave Canadian Threshold a second cheap creature that scaled with graveyards. The two threats asked different questions.

Tarmogoyf grew larger and could dominate creature combat, but ordinary removal could target it. Nimble Mongoose stopped at 3/3, but blanked much of the opponent’s spot removal. Sideboarding against one threat did not answer the other cleanly.

The eight-creature construction also maximized spell density. Every draw was likely to be a cantrip, counter, burn spell, or mana-denial piece, while eight redundant threats were enough to begin attacking early.

XIV · The Transformation

Delver changes the name, not the plan

Innistrad introduced Delver of Secrets, a one-mana blue creature capable of transforming into a 3/2 flier. Canadian Threshold quickly became RUG Delver.

The new threat attacked from a different angle and required no graveyard threshold, but it rewarded the same high instant-and-sorcery count. Lists commonly played four Delvers, four Mongooses, and several Tarmogoyfs.

The deck’s public name shifted toward its newest creature, yet the older Goose remained the purest expression of the plan: commit almost no mana to the battlefield, then spend every later card preventing the opponent from catching up.

XV · The Predator

The creature that kept the Goose small

Deathrite Shaman was uniquely hostile to Nimble Mongoose. Its one toughness became irrelevant because Mongoose could not target it or remove it, while Deathrite’s 1/2 body blocked the pre-threshold 1/1.

More importantly, Deathrite exiled cards from graveyards. It could delay threshold, remove the seventh card at a critical moment, and shrink an already enlarged Mongoose during combat.

Wizards banned Deathrite Shaman in Legacy in 2018 for broader metagame reasons. The removal of its incidental graveyard control helped traditional RUG strategies revisit the Goose, although later threats would again surpass it.

XVI · The Horizon

Modern Horizons opens another format

Modern Horizons reprinted Nimble Mongoose in 2019, adding it to the Modern card pool. The set also gave the card Kev Walker’s new artwork.

The legal change did not recreate Canadian Threshold. Modern lacked the exact combination of Brainstorm, Daze, Force of Will, Wasteland, and Stifle that made the Legacy shell oppressive, while graveyard hate was plentiful and creature threats had become more immediately powerful.

The reprint nevertheless confirmed the card’s historical stature. A creature once defined by Odyssey Standard and Legacy was selected as one of the past designs worthy of entering Modern directly.

XVII · The Common

The rarity downgrade

Eternal Masters reprinted Nimble Mongoose at common in 2016. When tabletop and digital Pauper legality were unified in 2019, that common printing made the creature legal in the format.

Pauper can reproduce parts of the old engine through Mental Note, Thought Scour, Brainstorm, Ponder, cheap counters, and burn. A one-mana shrouded 3/3 remains a meaningful rate.

The competition is substantial. Tolarian Terror, Gurmag Angler, graveyard hate, and more efficient contemporary engines often reward using or preserving graveyard cards differently. The Mongoose is legal and recognizable without becoming the format’s default green threat.

XVIII · The Keyword

Shroud gives way to hexproof

Nimble Mongoose originally spelled out its protection in full. Future Sight later introduced “shroud” as the keyword for the same rule, and Oracle text retroactively applied it to the Mongoose.

R&D eventually retired shroud from new designs in favor of hexproof. Playtesting showed that many players intuitively expected to be able to target their own protected creatures and found shroud’s self-denial disappointing.

Nimble Mongoose preserves the older philosophy. Its untargetability is not a pure benefit. The creature is independent of both players, forcing the deck to improve the game around it rather than the permanent itself.

XIX · The Image

Two artists, two kinds of speed

Terese Nielsen’s Odyssey artwork places the Mongoose inside a rushing field of green, brown, and white. The animal’s shape is visible but unstable, matching a creature whose defining defense is the inability to pin it down.

Kev Walker’s Eternal Masters and Modern Horizons image is clearer and more physical. The Mongoose races across a root or branch while predators and danger blur behind it, emphasizing speed rather than concealment.

The two versions express the same rules through different metaphors. Nielsen makes the creature difficult to perceive. Walker makes it impossible to catch.

XX · The Habitats

A life across formats

Historic Standard and Block

The threshold flagship

Le Wonder Goose and Dave Humpherys’s undefeated Worlds block deck used self-mill, discard, and Wonder to create an early 3/3 the opponent could not target.

Legacy

The original Canadian threat

Mongoose carried Canadian Threshold into RUG Delver and remains one of the clearest symbols of Legacy tempo.

Modern and Pauper

Legal through later reprints

Modern Horizons added it to Modern, while Eternal Masters’ common printing established Pauper legality.

Nimble Mongoose is currently legal in Modern, Legacy, Vintage, Commander, Pauper, and Oathbreaker. It is not legal in Standard, Pioneer, Historic, Timeless, or Brawl.

XXI · The Feeling

What players remember

The opponent had removal. The Goose had already decided that did not matter.
Nimble Mongoose made one green mana feel permanent and turned the seventh graveyard card into the moment a tempo deck’s clock became real.

Players remember announcing threshold, rotating the graveyard sideways, or arranging it into a visible stack of seven. They remember drawing a targeted removal spell and realizing it had no text against the creature already attacking.

They remember the contradiction: a deck filled with intricate cantrips, counters, fetches, and mana denial was ultimately winning with a small animal whose instructions were simply to arrive early and refuse to be touched.

XXIII · The Object

Printings and sources

Nimble Mongoose debuted as an Odyssey uncommon with Terese Nielsen’s artwork. Raphaël Lévy’s 2002 World Championship deck produced a gold-bordered commemorative version. Eternal Masters reprinted the card at common with new Kev Walker art, and Modern Horizons reused that image while adding the creature to Modern.
