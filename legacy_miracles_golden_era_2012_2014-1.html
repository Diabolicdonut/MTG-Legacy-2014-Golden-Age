Historical scope. This guide reconstructs Miracles as it existed from the release of Avacyn Restored in spring 2012 through the established U/W/r “Ponder Miracles” lists of August and early September 2014. The endpoint is deliberately placed before Khans of Tarkir and Treasure Cruise transformed blue Legacy. It is not a guide to current Legacy, and it assumes the card legality, rules, and opposing decks of that period.
1 · Cover and deck identity

Control the draw, control the game

Miracles was a blue-white control deck with a light red splash, but that description understates what made it special. It joined a conventional Legacy control core—Brainstorm, Force of Will, Swords to Plowshares and Jace, the Mind Sculptor—to a repeatable library-manipulation engine. Sensei’s Divining Top selected draws and staged miracle cards. Counterbalance converted knowledge of the top card into a soft lock. Terminus and Entreat the Angels turned a normally awkward timing restriction into an enormous mana advantage.

1 mana
Reset the battlefield.

Terminus answers almost every creature at once and places them on the bottom rather than in the graveyard.

2 cards
Form the prison engine.

Counterbalance plus Sensei’s Divining Top makes cheap spells unreliable and forces the opponent to fight on your terms.

1 turn
Convert defense into victory.

A protected Jace or a miracle Entreat can end a game that looked completely even one turn earlier.

The deck’s four identities

Draw-go control

Against Delver, tribal aggro, Maverick, Jund and most creature decks, Miracles is the pure control deck. It trades early, protects its mana, clears the board, then wins after the opponent’s pressure has been exhausted.

Soft-prison deck

Against cantrip-heavy blue decks and spell-based combo, Counterbalance is not merely value. With Top, it can deny broad bands of mana values and compel the opponent to spend resources before attempting a decisive spell.

Tap-out planeswalker deck

In fair, slow games, the goal becomes landing Jace with a clean battlefield and enough protection. Jace’s Brainstorm continuously improves Counterbalance coverage and puts stranded miracles back on top.

Flash threat deck

Post-board Vendilion Clique and end-step Entreat let Miracles pressure combo and control without surrendering its ability to interact. The deck can suddenly change from “do nothing” to a two-turn clock.

2 · What this deck does

A three-stage control machine

Miracles does not usually win by answering every card one-for-one. It wins by surviving until its cards begin answering entire classes of opposing cards. Terminus invalidates creature accumulation. CounterTop invalidates clusters of cheap spells. Jace invalidates ordinary draw steps. Entreat invalidates the opponent’s assumption that a stable board means a stable game.

Stabilize mana and information

Fetch basic Island, find Top or Ponder, identify whether the opponent is attacking your life total, your hand, your mana, or the stack. Avoid exposing Tundra or Volcanic Island without a reason.

Trade only where necessary

Swords, Force, Counterspell and Terminus are not interchangeable. Use the cheapest answer that preserves the resources needed for the opponent’s next axis of attack.

Establish an engine

Top plus fetchland improves every draw. Counterbalance plus Top constrains the opponent. Jace plus a stable board creates overwhelming selection. The engine matters more than an early clock.

Turn the corner decisively

Do not Entreat merely because you can. Cast it when the resulting clock is worth tapping mana, when it protects Jace, or when delaying gives the opponent meaningful outs.

Why the shell fit the 2012–2014 Legacy card pool

Legacy’s best decks were heavily concentrated at one and two mana. Delver shells, Storm, Elves, Stoneblade and tribal decks all relied on efficient spells. That made Counterbalance unusually broad. At the same time, creature strategies frequently relied on protection, recursion or resilient board development; Terminus ignored indestructibility, shroud and death triggers because it neither targeted nor destroyed. The same Top that enabled these cards also made a 21-land control deck function with remarkable consistency.

The central exchange: Miracles spends small amounts of mana and time arranging the library, then receives disproportionate effects. The opponent’s opportunity is to exploit the setup cost—through Wasteland, Stifle, Abrupt Decay, discard, fast combo, a planeswalker, or the tournament clock—before the engine becomes self-sustaining.
3 · History and evolution

From experimental shells to the format’s reference control deck

April–May 2012 · Avacyn Restored

Miracle becomes a tournament mechanic

Terminus and Entreat the Angels immediately offered effects Legacy control wanted, but at costs that became absurd when the top of the library could be engineered. The older Counterbalance–Top package supplied exactly that engineering. Brainstorm, Jace and fetchlands completed the bridge between the two engines.

July 2012 · Grand Prix Ghent

The archetype arrives before its list is solved

Max Sjöblom and Andrea Milillo both reached the Top 8 of the 1,345-player Legacy Grand Prix. Their lists show how open the archetype still was. Sjöblom played a black splash, Vedalken Shackles and a mixed miracle count. Milillo played 61 cards, Standstill, Mishra’s Factory, Elspeth and only two Swords to Plowshares alongside two Path to Exile. The strategic core was visible, but “the Miracles 75” did not yet exist.

2012–2013 · The refinement period

Control pilots test every surrounding shell

Lists explored black discard, Rest in Peace packages, Standstill, Helm of Obedience, Moat, extra Cliques, more lands, fewer cantrips and various removal suites. The stable center remained Top, Brainstorm, Force, Counterbalance, Terminus and Jace. The question was how much of the deck should be devoted to making that center consistent.

Late 2013 · True-Name Nemesis

Sweepers and non-targeting answers gain value

True-Name Nemesis strengthened UWR Delver and Stoneblade while making ordinary spot-removal packages less reliable. Terminus was already the cleanest answer. Engineered Explosives, red blasts and later Council’s Judgment gave Miracles additional post-board tools without changing its primary identity.

February 2014 · Grand Prix Paris

Miracles becomes a pillar, not a curiosity

Miracles was the second-most represented archetype on Day 2 and placed three pilots in the Top 8. Philipp Schönegger finished in the Top 4. His report and later primer describe a deck that had favorable play across the top tables and rewarded specialist knowledge.

Summer 2014 · Ponder Miracles

Consistency wins the internal debate

The streamlined build cut down to 21 lands, removed narrow maindeck creatures, maximized Ponder and Counterbalance, and added a flexible Council’s Judgment. It was less interested in drawing a particular silver bullet and more interested in seeing enough cards to assemble the correct answer package every game.

September 2014 · The cutoff

Treasure Cruise opens a different Legacy era

Khans of Tarkir introduced delve decks capable of rebuilding with a one-mana draw-three. The resulting Delver metagame belongs to the next historical chapter. This guide stops just before that inflection point.

Perfect hindsight — my final pre–Treasure Cruise 75

My conclusion is deliberately conservative: the August Ponder Miracles maindeck was already the correct endpoint. Perfect hindsight does not justify weakening its consistency to revive Standstill, Helm, black discard, maindeck blasts or the Karakas–Venser package. I would register the same 60 and change one sideboard slot: Pithing Needle over the third Counterspell.

Perfect-Hindsight MiraclesMy recommended 75 for the late-summer 2014 pre-Cruise gauntlet +

The single change: Pithing Needle is less powerful in a pure counter war than the third Counterspell, but it covers more of the actual gauntlet: Sensei’s Divining Top and Jace in the mirror, Aether Vial and Rishadan Port, Sneak Attack and Griselbrand, Grindstone, Liliana, Wasteland, equipment activations and miscellaneous prison pieces. Ponder makes a one-of broad answer realistic. The rest of the sideboard remains disciplined: three blasts for blue, two Flusterstorms and two Cliques for combo/control, two Rest in Peace, two Explosives, and three distinct permanent trumps.

What hindsight rejects

Standstill asks the deck to support Factories and awkward board states. Enlightened Tutor creates card disadvantage and increases the number of narrow natural draws. Black discard damages the mana base. Karakas–Venser is powerful but consumes too many slots for how infrequently the complete loop matters.

What hindsight confirms

Four Ponder, four Counterbalance, four Terminus, three Snapcaster and 21 lands are not merely streamlined aesthetics. They minimize the deck’s failure modes while retaining enough broad answers to play against every major strategy of the period.

Card packages in the final build

12

Library manipulation

4 Top, 4 Brainstorm, 4 Ponder. The package finds lands, prevents miracle cards from becoming dead draws, sets Counterbalance, and turns narrow sideboard cards into realistic one- and two-ofs.

10

Stack interaction

4 Force, 2 Counterspell, 4 Counterbalance. Force bridges the dangerous opening; Counterspell governs the middle turns; Counterbalance converts setup into recurring advantage.

9

Permanent control

4 Swords, 4 Terminus, 1 Judgment. Swords handles exact threats, Terminus punishes development and bypasses protection, and Judgment answers planeswalkers or otherwise awkward permanents.

8

Advantage and victory

3 Snapcaster, 3 Jace, 2 Entreat. Snapcaster stretches the cheap interaction. Jace controls future draw steps. Entreat produces the fastest clean finish without occupying many slots.

5 · Mulligans and opening turns

Keep functional plans, not collections of powerful cards

A Miracles opening hand should answer three questions: Can it develop mana? Can it see more cards? Can it survive the opponent’s fastest relevant line? A hand with Jace, Entreat, Terminus and Force may look powerful while doing almost nothing. A hand with Island, fetchland, Top, Ponder and Swords may contain no explicit victory condition but is exactly what the deck wants.

General keeps

  • Two lands plus Top or Ponder, with at least one relevant interaction spell.
  • One land plus multiple cheap looks when the land casts them and the matchup is not heavily punishing a stumble.
  • Basic-heavy mana against known Wasteland decks, even when the hand is less glamorous.
  • Top plus Counterbalance when the hand can cast both on schedule and will not lose immediately while doing so.
  • Force plus blue card and selection against unknown opponents or fast combo, provided the hand can still develop after pitching a card.

Common mulligans

  • No-land hands and most one-land hands without Top, Brainstorm or Ponder.
  • Hands whose only land is a dual against a known Wasteland deck, especially without additional selection.
  • Multiple miracles without Brainstorm, Jace or Top.
  • Reactive hands that cannot cast their answers in the required window.
  • Hands with only expensive finishers and Force of Will: pitching the only functional card often leaves nothing.
Top is not automatically a keep. Top improves a functioning hand; it does not create mana. A one-land Top hand that misses its second land may spend turns looking at the same three cards. A fetchland makes that hand much better because it provides a shuffle.

Brainstorm discipline in the opening turns

Do not cast Brainstorm merely because one blue mana is unused. Its best early jobs are fixing a genuinely bad hand, hiding key cards from discard, returning a miracle to the library, or responding to a shuffle opportunity. A turn-one Ponder is often the cleaner development spell. Brainstorm becomes stronger as the game reveals which cards are expendable and as fetchlands give it a reliable reset.

6 · Key interactions

The mechanics that make Miracles more than “blue-white good cards”

Counterbalance and Sensei’s Divining Top

When the opponent casts a spell, Counterbalance triggers. Before that trigger resolves, activate Top to inspect and rearrange the top three cards. Counterbalance then reveals the chosen top card. If its mana value matches the spell, that spell is countered. Legacy’s density of one- and two-mana spells makes a small number of known cards cover a large portion of the format.

Top itself can become a one-mana card on top of the library: activate its draw ability in response to the Counterbalance trigger. You draw a card and place Top on top, allowing Counterbalance to reveal it to counter a spell with mana value one. The cost is losing immediate Top access until the next draw or shuffle sequence.

Top, fetchlands and the “six-card” search

Top sees three cards. If none is useful, crack a fetchland and see three different cards. This is not literal card advantage, but it radically improves card quality. Conversely, do not shuffle away a mapped top three without a reason. Miracles rewards remembering the order and planning which draw step, Top draw or fetchland will access each card.

Creating a miracle on the opponent’s turn

Miracle checks the first card you draw in each turn, not just your turn. If you have not drawn a card during the opponent’s turn, place Terminus or Entreat on top with Top, then activate Top’s draw ability. Reveal the miracle before mixing it into your hand. The trigger goes on the stack; when it resolves, you may cast the card for its miracle cost even though it is a sorcery.

Priority matters. An opponent may respond to the miracle trigger. Stifle can counter it. If that happens, the revealed card remains in your hand and cannot later be cast for its miracle cost from that draw.

Brainstorm and Jace as miracle storage

Drawing Terminus or Entreat normally is not fatal. Brainstorm places it back on top, after which a Top draw can cast it during the opponent’s turn or the normal draw step can cast it on yours. Jace’s zero ability performs the same repair repeatedly. This is why “dead miracles in hand” are often delayed resources rather than true blanks.

Mana value does not become the miracle cost

Miracle is an alternative cost. It does not change a card’s mana value. Terminus remains mana value six for Counterbalance even when cast for one white. Entreat the Angels has mana value three while it is in the library because X is zero outside the stack. Once Entreat is cast, the chosen X contributes to its mana value on the stack.

Counterbalance’s less obvious hits

  • A land has mana value zero, so a revealed land can counter a zero-mana spell such as a suspended Ancestral Vision when it is cast.
  • Terminus provides a six, occasionally countering expensive spells that most Counterbalance decks could not cover.
  • Force of Will provides a five even if it is cast for its alternative cost.
  • Jace and Counterbalance provide fours and twos, while Snapcaster, Counterspell and the broad two-mana package make two easy to cover.

Terminus versus ordinary sweepers

Terminus does not target and does not destroy. It handles True-Name Nemesis, Nimble Mongoose, creatures protected by Mother of Runes, indestructible creatures, and recursive threats without triggering ordinary “dies” abilities. The drawback is timing: drawing it naturally without setup is poor, and casting it too early may permit the opponent to rebuild before your engine is ready.

Snapcaster Mage’s limits

Snapcaster gives a card flashback equal to its mana cost, not its miracle cost. A Terminus in the graveyard costs 4WW through flashback. Snapcaster is principally additional Brainstorms, Ponders, Swords, Counterspells and sideboard interaction. It also provides a flash blocker or a small clock after the game becomes about protecting a planeswalker.

Entreat timing

An end-step Entreat is usually superior to a main-phase Entreat because it preserves interaction and gives the opponent less time to answer the tokens before they attack. But the correct X is strategic, not maximal. Two Angels can be enough to close while retaining Counterspell. Four Angels may be necessary to race. One Angel can protect Jace or ambush a Delver when mana is constrained.

7 · How to pilot the deck

Think in future draw steps, not isolated turns

The deck’s real resource is not merely cards in hand. It is controlled access to the correct card at the correct point in the turn cycle.

1. Identify the axis that can actually beat you

Against RUG Delver, the danger is often mana denial plus a modest clock, not any single creature. Against Shardless BUG, it is planeswalker and card-advantage accumulation. Against Storm, it is a protected stack turn. Against Death & Taxes, it is the combination of mana pressure and a permanent that makes your answers clumsy. Choose plays to defeat the axis, not the most visible card.

2. Build mana before displaying power

A basic Island, a basic Plains and a Top may look passive, but they make the rest of the deck castable through Wasteland. Fetch Volcanic Island only when a red spell is going to matter. Fetch the second white source before you need to hard-cast Terminus or cast Council’s Judgment through disruption. The strongest spell in hand is irrelevant when the land sequence cannot cast it.

3. Use Top with a purpose

Repeatedly activating Top without changing a decision wastes mana and tournament time. Activate when you need to select a draw, set Counterbalance, stage a miracle, respond to discard, or decide whether to fetch. Maintain a mental map of the top three. Announce actions cleanly and resolve them at a consistent pace.

4. Make cantrips solve roles

Do not ask, “What is the strongest card in these three?” Ask, “What must my next two turns accomplish?” You may need a basic land more than Jace, a blue card for Force more than Entreat, or a one-mana card on top more than the card you intend to draw. In a Counterbalance game, the order of cards can matter more than their absolute quality.

5. Let the opponent commit into Terminus—within reason

The ideal Terminus answers several cards, but greed loses games. Calculate damage, Daze, burn, equipment and haste. If taking one more hit increases the sweep from one creature to three without exposing you to a realistic loss, wait. If the opponent can protect the next threat or shut off your setup, take the safe reset.

6. Treat Force of Will as emergency architecture

Force is for spells that invalidate the game plan or arrive before normal interaction is online: a lethal combo spell, a planeswalker that will dominate, a critical Counterbalance in the mirror, or a mana-denial piece that makes the hand collapse. Against attrition decks, indiscriminate Force use turns their ordinary card into a two-for-one.

7. Know the turning point

Miracles can lose by remaining defensive after it has gained control. Once the opponent’s meaningful routes are covered, use Clique, Snapcaster attacks, Jace fateseal or Entreat to shorten the game. This is particularly important against combo, inevitability engines such as Punishing Fire, and the round clock.

8. Play the match clock as a real resource

Top creates many legal decisions, but the pilot must make them efficiently. Shuffle promptly, communicate Top order clearly, prepare sideboarding before the event, and concede games that are functionally unwinnable. A theoretically favorable matchup is not favorable when every match ends 1–0 or in a draw.

A compact decision loop

Before the turn

What is on top? What is the opponent’s best line? Which land must remain untapped?

At each spell

Must this resolve? Which answer preserves the most important future card? Does Counterbalance need help?

At end step

Should Top draw, Entreat, fetch, Brainstorm, or simply preserve known cards for the next draw?

8 · Sideboarding principles

Change the texture of the deck without damaging its engine

Force of Will is matchup-dependent, not sacred

Keep Force when the opponent can produce an early, game-ending spell or a permanent you cannot profitably answer after resolution. Trim or remove it against fair attrition decks where trading two cards for one ordinary threat is exactly what the opponent wants. RUG Delver is a historical example where Schönegger removed all four Forces and relied on efficient removal, Rest in Peace, Flusterstorm and permanent answers.

Counterbalance is powerful but not universally durable

Counterbalance is at its best against Storm, cantrip decks and opponents whose curve is concentrated at one and two. It becomes less reliable against Abrupt Decay decks, Aether Vial decks, Cavern of Souls, varied permanent curves, or strategies that fight mostly through lands. Do not keep four merely because it is the deck’s namesake card.

Preserve enough blue cards

When Force stays in, count the remaining blue cards after sideboarding. Removing Ponders, Counterbalances and Jaces while adding white or artifact cards can leave Force stranded. This matters especially against combo, where a seven-card hand with Force but no pitch is often a mulligan.

Matchup heuristics that transfer across the gauntlet

  • Wasteland + soft counters: prioritize basics and avoid casting into Daze merely to use mana.
  • Abrupt Decay: Counterbalance becomes a temporary obstacle, not a complete plan; lean into Jace, Entreat, Clique and Keranos.
  • Aether Vial or Cavern: reduce narrow counters and increase board control.
  • Fast combo: retain blue density and combine Force with permanent disruption and a clock.
  • Graveyard decks: Rest in Peace is an engine answer, but do not keep a hand that dies before casting it.
  • Control mirrors: remove dead creature interaction, fight over Top/Jace, and convert small tempo advantages into a clock.
10 · Historical notes and sources

Why this period is remembered as a Golden Era

The label does not mean Miracles was uncontested or universally loved. It means the archetype had reached a rare balance of identity, power and specialist depth. Its central cards were stable; its flex slots still mattered; its matchups rewarded experience; and the opposing field included Delver, Stoneblade, Shardless BUG, Death & Taxes, Elves, Storm, Show and Tell, Reanimator and tribal strategies with sharply different demands.

Grand Prix Ghent showed that the miracle cards belonged in Legacy before deck construction had converged. Grand Prix Paris showed the converged strategy could occupy three Top 8 seats in a large, mature field. Schönegger’s 2014 primer then captured the deck at the moment its internal logic was most explicit: four Ponder, four Counterbalance, a disciplined mana base and matchup plans built around role identification rather than generic “good cards.”

What happened later

Treasure Cruise and delve reshaped the blue tempo decks immediately after this guide’s cutoff. Miracles adapted and remained a premier strategy for years, later incorporating cards such as Monastery Mentor. In April 2017, Wizards banned Sensei’s Divining Top in Legacy, explicitly citing both Miracles’ sustained position as the format’s best deck and the tournament-time burden of repeated Top activations. That later ban confirms how central the CounterTop engine remained, but it should not be read backward into every 2012 list: the first two years were a period of discovery.
