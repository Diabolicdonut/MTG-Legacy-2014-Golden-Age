I · The Barrier

The Empty Hand Became a Wall

Ensnaring Bridge turns a player’s lack of options into physical security. Five cards in hand permit creatures with power five or less to attack. One card allows only creatures with power one or less. No cards means that every creature with positive power must remain behind the structure.

The paradox is complete. Card advantage usually protects a player by providing more answers. Ensnaring Bridge protects its controller by removing those answers from hand. The safest state is not abundance but emptiness.

That inversion produced prison decks, discard decks, artifact combo decks, Vintage Workshop shells, Painter toolboxes, and eventually Lantern Control—the Modern archetype that used complete knowledge of the opponent’s next draw to ensure the Bridge would never be answered.

The original object

Ensnaring Bridge

Creatures with power greater than the number of cards in your hand can’t attack.

II · The Passage

Stronghold’s inverted defense

Stronghold completed the story and mechanics of the Rath cycle with cards designed around confined spaces, attrition, and escape. Ensnaring Bridge made the setting literal: the battlefield contained a route that became narrower as its controller possessed fewer resources.

Unlike Moat, Meekstone, or Propaganda, the Bridge does not define a permanent category that is always excluded. Its threshold moves with a hidden zone.

The controller can alter the rule through ordinary play. Casting a spell, discarding a card, cycling, activating a discard outlet, or resolving a draw spell may immediately change which creatures are allowed to attack.

Ensnaring Bridge asks the control player to become hellbent so the battlefield cannot become violent.Fewer options converted into fewer attackers
III · The Number

Hand size becomes the gate

The Bridge compares each creature’s current power with the number of cards in the Bridge controller’s hand.

Three cards

Power three or less may attack

A Tarmogoyf with four power is stopped, while a three-power creature remains legal.

One card

Only power one or less may attack

Small utility creatures and zero-power attackers may still cross.

Zero cards

Positive power cannot attack

A creature must have power zero or less at the declaration moment to become an attacker.

The comparison uses “greater than,” not “greater than or equal to.” A creature whose power exactly matches the hand size can attack.

IV · The Restriction

What Ensnaring Bridge actually does

Ensnaring Bridge creates a static attacking restriction. It applies to every creature controlled by every player, including the Bridge controller.

The restriction does not target. Hexproof, shroud, protection, and ward do not help a creature attack through it. The artifact does not tap creatures, prevent blocking, remove abilities, or affect combat already in progress.

If several Bridges are on the battlefield, each creates a separate restriction using its own controller’s hand. A creature must satisfy all applicable restrictions to be declared as an attacker.

Power and hand size are dynamic. Both values are checked only during the process of declaring attackers. The Bridge does not remember what they were earlier in the turn.
Pete Venters’s original Ensnaring BridgeThe bridge is not merely narrow. It curves through a predatory structure whose stone and metal seem prepared to close around anything attempting passage.
V · The Declaration

The check happens while attackers are declared

Ensnaring Bridge checks power and hand size while a player is declaring attackers. The game first determines which creatures can legally attack, then the declaration is completed.

Once a creature has legally become an attacker, increasing its power or reducing the Bridge controller’s hand does not remove it from combat. The moment of permission has passed.

Conversely, drawing a card before attackers are declared may open the bridge to larger creatures. Discarding a card before declaration may close it.

VI · The Emptiness

What zero cards means

With no cards in the Bridge controller’s hand, creatures with power greater than zero cannot attack. Most creatures are therefore stopped.

A creature with zero power can still be declared as an attacker. Negative power also satisfies the restriction, although such a creature ordinarily deals no combat damage without later modification.

This zero-card state is the goal of Lantern Control, 8-Rack, and many prison decks. The controller deliberately spends or discards every card because the final card is worth less than turning off the opponent’s entire combat phase.

VII · The Trick

Attack small, become large later

A creature may attack if its power is low enough at declaration and become larger afterward. The Bridge does not recheck it later in combat.

Signal Pest can attack with zero power under an empty-hand Bridge, then battle cry increases the power of other attacking creatures. Exalted triggers can enlarge the single creature that was already declared. Cranial Plating can be moved or activated after declaration.

This creates a classic escape route: satisfy the Bridge with a harmless body, then make that body dangerous once it is already across.

VIII · The Bypass

Creatures placed onto the battlefield attacking

Some effects put a creature onto the battlefield attacking without declaring it as an attacker. Because Ensnaring Bridge restricts declaration rather than the attacking state itself, those creatures can enter combat.

The same principle applies to effects that create attacking tokens or cause a creature to become attacking outside the normal declaration procedure.

The distinction is narrow but important. “Can’t attack” in this context means “can’t be declared as an attacker,” not “can never be an attacking creature.”

IX · The Controller

The Bridge restrains its controller too

Ensnaring Bridge does not say “creatures your opponents control.” A prison deck that plans to win through large attackers may trap its own finishers.

Successful Bridge decks choose win conditions that do not need combat, can attack at low power and grow later, can remove the Bridge on demand, or can wait until the controller refills the hand.

This self-restriction is part of the design’s price. The artifact is universally castable, but the controller must build around the same wall imposed on everyone else.

X · The Hand

Why discarding your own hand is an advantage

Traditional control wants more cards because each card represents another answer. Bridge control wants enough cards to establish the battlefield and then wants those cards gone.

Low mana curves make that possible. Zero- and one-mana artifacts, discard spells, mana rocks, and activated abilities allow the deck to deploy nearly everything quickly.

Cards that discard as a cost become doubly useful. They perform their printed function while reducing the maximum power allowed to attack.

XI · The Schedule

Bottled Cloister removes the hand on schedule

Bottled Cloister exiles its controller’s hand at the beginning of each opponent’s upkeep and returns those cards during the controller’s upkeep before drawing an additional card.

During the opponent’s combat phase, the Bridge controller therefore has zero cards in hand and Ensnaring Bridge stops every creature with positive power. During the controller’s own turn, the hand returns and can be used normally.

The interaction is powerful and dangerous. If Bottled Cloister leaves the battlefield while the cards are exiled, they remain exiled. Vintage Stax accepted that risk because the scheduled empty hand made the combat lock nearly automatic.

XII · The Attrition

8-Rack closes both hands

Modern 8-Rack wants the opponent empty-handed for The Rack and Shrieking Affliction. Its own hand also tends to disappear through cheap discard, Smallpox effects, and Liliana of the Veil.

Ensnaring Bridge turns that shared scarcity into asymmetrical safety. The opponent’s empty hand causes damage. The controller’s empty hand prevents combat.

The deck can win without attacking at all. Rack triggers continue reducing life while Bridge makes creature removal and battlefield size increasingly irrelevant.

XIII · The Information

Lantern Control separates drawing from choice

Lantern of Insight reveals the top card of each library. Codex Shredder, Ghoulcaller’s Bell, and related mill artifacts decide whether an undesirable opposing draw remains on top.

Ensnaring Bridge buys the turns required for that informational lock. Creature decks cannot simply attack while Lantern assembles; once the controller empties the hand, combat stops.

The deck does not counter every spell. It attempts to ensure that the opponent never draws the relevant spell in the first place. The Bridge protects the engine from the cards already on the battlefield.

XIV · The Champion

The Grand Prix champion nobody could attack

Zac Elsik won Grand Prix Oklahoma City in 2015 with four main-deck Ensnaring Bridges. The victory transformed Lantern Control from an eccentric brew into a premier-event champion.

Elsik’s list contained only three creatures, all Spellskites. Twenty-six other nonland permanents were cheap artifacts and a single Ghirapur Aether Grid. The deck could empty its hand rapidly and protect the Bridge from targeted removal.

Once combat was closed, the opponent’s library became the battlefield that mattered. Lantern revealed the next card, mill effects removed answers, and Academy Ruins returned destroyed or used artifacts to the top of the deck.

XV · The Workshop

The Bridge inside Workshop prison

Vintage Stax attacks mana, permanents, and time through Mishra's Workshop, Sphere effects, Tangle Wire, Smokestack, Wasteland, and Crucible of Worlds.

Ensnaring Bridge addresses the creatures that enter before the prison fully closes or survive the resource exchange. Workshop mana casts it quickly, and the deck’s low card velocity naturally keeps hand size small.

Mishra’s Factory and small artifact creatures can remain useful beneath the threshold, while Bottled Cloister supplies the most complete version of the lock during opposing turns.

XVI · The Toolbox

The tutorable emergency wall

Legacy Painter decks commonly use one Ensnaring Bridge as a searchable prison piece. Enlightened Tutor, Goblin Engineer, Goblin Welder, and Karn, the Great Creator can find or recur it.

The Bridge buys time against creature decks while Painter’s Servant and Grindstone assemble a noncombat win. Small creatures such as Goblin Welder can continue attacking if hand size permits, but combat damage is rarely the primary plan.

As a singleton, the artifact need not be universally good. It must be devastating in the matchup where the toolbox chooses it.

XVII · The Wish

Karn turns the sideboard into a draw step

Karn, the Great Creator can retrieve an artifact from outside the game in tournament play, normally the sideboard. This allows prison and Tron decks to keep Ensnaring Bridge outside the main deck until combat becomes the problem that must be solved.

The arrangement reduces dead draws against combo and control while making the Bridge available through every Karn.

Karn can also shut down opposing artifacts and later find Liquimetal Coating, Mycosynth Lattice, or a separate win condition. The Bridge protects the planeswalker while the wish-board engine takes over.

XVIII · The Conclusion

How a deck wins from behind the Bridge

The artifact prevents many attacks but does not reduce an opponent’s life or library. A successful Bridge deck must end the game without relying on creatures too large to cross.

Damage without combat

Rack effects, Grid, and Spellbomb

Triggered damage and activated artifacts ignore the combat restriction.

Library control

Lantern and Grindstone

Mill engines win through an empty library rather than an open attack lane.

Planeswalkers

Karn, Chandra, and other engines

The Bridge protects loyalty while planeswalkers produce cards, damage, or a separate lock.

The card is strongest when the win condition uses the same low-hand or artifact infrastructure required to maintain the prison.

XIX · The Crossing

How opponents cross it

Artifact removal is the direct answer. Abrupt Decay, Force of Vigor, Boseiju, Who Endures, Prismatic Ending, and numerous sideboard cards can remove the structure before combat.

Players can also increase the Bridge controller’s hand with forced draw, attack with zero-power creatures and pump them later, put creatures onto the battlefield attacking, use noncombat damage, mill, planeswalkers, or alternate wins.

The most reliable approach is strategic diversity. A deck whose only route to victory is declaring large attackers may become completely dependent on drawing one specific class of answer.

XX · The Pressure

Why the card became less absolute

Modern threats increasingly generate value before combat. Creatures draw cards, destroy permanents, create tokens, or operate through activated abilities. Preventing attacks no longer neutralizes every reason those creatures were played.

Artifact removal has also become broader and more maindeckable. Boseiju, Prismatic Ending, Haywire Mite, Karn, and flexible modal cards make a three-mana artifact harder to treat as an untouchable foundation.

The Bridge still invalidates combat with unmatched efficiency. It simply exists in a game where more decks can win or gain decisive value without crossing it.

XXI · The Table

The multiplayer hand-size problem

In Commander, Ensnaring Bridge compares every attacking creature with one player’s hand: the hand of the Bridge controller. Emptying that hand can stop combat from three opponents simultaneously.

That controller must also function with very few cards available. Artifact commanders, hellbent strategies, graveyard engines, planeswalkers, and commanders that generate value from the command zone can compensate.

The political result is immediate. Creature decks may cooperate to remove the Bridge, while combo and control players may prefer that the artifact remain because it redirects pressure away from them.

XXII · The Bloodline

The descendants and relatives

Meekstone prevents large creatures from untapping after they become tapped. Moat excludes nonfliers. Ghostly Prison taxes each attacker. Silent Arbiter limits how many creatures can attack.

The same machine, reimagined

Masters 25 Ensnaring Bridge

Titus Lunter’s artwork turns the Bridge into a massive closing mechanism, making the rules text visually explicit: the larger the creature attempting to pass, the more completely the structure encloses it.

Crawlspace permits only two attacking creatures. No Mercy destroys attackers after they deal damage. Glacial Chasm prevents damage while forbidding its controller from attacking.

None uses a hidden-zone count as directly. Ensnaring Bridge remains the prison card whose strength can be adjusted simply by playing the cards already in hand.

XXIII · The Image

Three visions of a hostile passage

Pete Venters’s Stronghold image depicts a bridge integrated into a clawed, skeletal structure. The route itself appears alive and predatory.

Ron Spencer’s Seventh Edition replacement makes the danger more anatomical, surrounding the passage with a mouthlike architecture and descending mechanisms. Florian de Gesincourt’s Kaladesh Invention turns it into a gilded Ghirapur machine whose beauty disguises its function.

Masters 25 and Double Masters use Titus Lunter’s enormous mechanical trap. Tales of Middle-earth Commander reinterprets the card as the Bridge of Khazad-dûm, a narrow passage that controls which creatures can cross through physical scale rather than machinery.

XXIV · The Habitats

A life across formats

Modern

Lantern, 8-Rack, Karn, and prison

Core-set printings made the card legal from Modern’s beginning, where it became a defining combat lock and toolbox target.

Legacy and Vintage

Toolbox and Workshop infrastructure

Painter, Stax, MUD, mono-red prison, and Karn shells use the Bridge as a searchable or accelerated answer to creatures.

Commander

A global combat restriction

One empty hand can restrain three opposing battlefields, provided the controller can operate without keeping resources in hand.

Ensnaring Bridge is currently legal in Modern, Legacy, Vintage, Commander, and Oathbreaker. It is not legal in Standard, Pioneer, Historic, Timeless, Brawl, or Pauper.

XXV · The Feeling

What players remember

The controller had nothing left. That was why nobody could attack.
Ensnaring Bridge made an empty hand look less like defeat and more like a locked gate whose key had been deliberately discarded.

Players remember counting the opponent’s hand before combat, then watching the final card leave it. They remember the one-power creature that could cross, the pump spell cast after declaration, and the Bridge player drawing one unwanted card that suddenly opened the battlefield.

They remember Lantern Control revealing every future answer and milling each one away. The artifact did not merely prevent an attack. It made the possibility of attacking depend on whether the defender still possessed choices.

XXVII · The Object

Printings and sources

Ensnaring Bridge debuted in Stronghold with Pete Venters’s artwork. Seventh Edition replaced the original image with Ron Spencer’s interpretation and introduced the card’s first foil. Kaladesh Inventions gave it a premium Florian de Gesincourt treatment, while Masters 25 introduced Titus Lunter’s modern art. Tales of Middle-earth Commander later reskinned the card as the Bridge of Khazad-dûm.
