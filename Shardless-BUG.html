Shardless BUG

Legacy’s cascade midrange engine: trade resources early, then turn a three-mana 2/2 into Ancestral Recall while Deathrite, Tarmogoyf, planeswalkers, and uncounterable removal bury every fair opponent.

Historical scope. This guide begins with the first serious Shardless Agent control experiments in summer 2012 and follows the archetype through September 2014, immediately before Treasure Cruise. It treats the post–Return to Ravnica Deathrite Shaman/Abrupt Decay shell as the canonical deck while preserving the earlier Deed-and-Top lineage.
1 · Cover and deck identity

The fairest unfair thing in Legacy

Shardless BUG wins with creatures, discard, removal, and planeswalkers—the ordinary tools of a midrange deck. Its unfair element is the way those cards replace themselves. Shardless Agent always produces another spell. Ancestral Vision draws three. Baleful Strix trades with a creature after drawing. Deathrite Shaman is mana, graveyard disruption, life gain, and reach. The opponent can answer each permanent and still fall behind.

0 mana
Draw three cards.

Cascade ignores Ancestral Vision’s absent mana cost and casts it without paying.

4 Decays
Most cheap permanents are temporary.

Counterbalance, Delver, equipment, Vial, Goyf, and hate permanents can be answered without fear of countermagic.

1 Shaman
Hold the whole deck together.

Deathrite fixes three colors, accelerates Agent and Jace, checks graveyards, and ends stalled games.

The deck’s four identities

Attrition midrange

Against Delver, Stoneblade, Jund, Maverick, and other fair decks, Shardless trades cards and relies on Vision, Agent, Strix, and planeswalkers to win the resource count.

Tap-out control

Against Miracles and slow decks, it develops lands, suspends Vision, removes engines with Decay, and lands Jace or Liliana behind expendable creatures.

Discard-plus-clock combo opponent

Against Storm, Show and Tell, and Reanimator, the fair cards become liabilities. Thoughtseize, Hymn, Force, Deathrite, and Tarmogoyf must create a narrow window to win.

Board-control engine

Strix, Goyf, Decay, Liliana, Pulse, Charm, and Deluge let the deck reshape creature battlefields while its cascade cards keep the hand full.

2 · What this deck does

Trade, cascade, overwhelm

Shardless BUG seeks games where both players exchange cards early. Thoughtseize, Hymn, Force, Decay, Strix, and Liliana reduce the board and hands. The crucial asymmetry is that the Shardless player has suspended Visions, Agents, creature-lands, Deathrite activations, and planeswalkers waiting to refill the game.

Stabilize mana first

The deck needs black on turn one, double black for Hymn or Liliana, green and blue for Agent, and blue-blue for Jace. Fetch sequencing is a strategic resource.

Exchange efficiently

Decay the permanent that unlocks several opposing cards, discard the spell your deck cannot answer, and save Force for effects that invalidate ordinary play.

Create delayed advantage

Suspending Vision asks the opponent to win before it resolves. Shardless Agent shortens that delay and adds a body that attacks planeswalkers.

Close before unfair decks rebuild

Against combo, the deck cannot merely accumulate cards. Goyf, Deathrite, Tar Pit, and Agents must turn disruption into a finite clock.

The central bargain: Shardless accepts less precise cascades and some awkward game-one cards in exchange for being exceptionally difficult to exhaust. It is strongest when the format’s best decks expect to win by trading resources.
3 · History and evolution

From cascade experiment to midrange benchmark

June–August 2012 · The first brews

Agent meets Ancestral Vision

Brian DeMars built around the interaction immediately. The first shell used Top, Jace, Brainstorm, Snapcaster, Pernicious Deed, basics, and traditional removal. It was BUG control first and a refined cascade deck second.

October–December 2012 · Return to Ravnica

Deathrite Shaman and Abrupt Decay solve the architecture

Deathrite accelerated Agent and Jace while fixing the demanding mana. Abrupt Decay replaced narrower removal and attacked Counterbalance. Gerry Thompson’s December Invitational list already contained the permanent core recognizable throughout 2013–2014.

January 2013 · GP Denver

The deck chooses control over tempo

Twenty-three lands, only two Forces, four Thoughtseizes, and three Jaces made the philosophy explicit. Wasteland was a utility answer, not a reason to sacrifice land development.

June 2013 · Baleful Strix

The fourteen-creature shell emerges

Frank Skarren’s SCG Baltimore Top 4 list added two Strixes and demonstrated that the archetype could be piloted successfully even without years of Legacy-specific experience.

November 2013 · Grand Prix Washington, D.C.

Another Grand Prix Top 8

Ted McCluskie placed seventh in the same event won by UWR Delver. The archetype was now a known foil to Stoneblade, Miracles, and fair blue decks rather than an experimental strategy.

Late 2013–March 2014 · True-Name Nemesis

The untargetable creature changes the planeswalker balance

Abrupt Decay and Pulse could not remove True-Name itself. Lists reduced Jace, increased Liliana, and adopted Golgari Charm, Toxic Deluge, and Night of Souls’ Betrayal—cards useful beyond the single matchup.

Summer 2014 · A trophy-converting pillar

Strix counts and sideboards diversify

Some lists played four Strix; others retained two and more planeswalkers. The deck was widely regarded as a premier answer to the established fair metagame, with combo and Blood Moon remaining the central risks.

September 2014 · The mature primer

The 2/2 Jace–Liliana split becomes the reference

Gerry Thompson’s final pre-Cruise primer presented a balanced open-field list with eleven fetchlands, a basic Swamp, two Wastelands, flexible removal, and a modular sideboard.

September 2014 · The cutoff

Treasure Cruise alters the attrition equation

Shardless’s advantage was that it refilled more effectively than other fair decks. A one-mana draw-three available to Delver strategies fundamentally changed that comparison.

5 · Mulligans and opening turns

Keep mana, purpose, and a plan for the first crisis

Shardless is powerful but not forgiving of mana failure. A hand with Agent, Liliana, Jace, and Vision can be rich in card quality while functionally doing nothing. The first requirement is a sequence of colored lands; the second is an early play that addresses the matchup’s speed.

General keeps

  • Deathrite plus two lands, especially when the lands or fetches provide black and a second color.
  • Turn-one suspended Vision with removal or discard to survive until it resolves.
  • Two or three lands, Brainstorm, and interaction against unknown opponents.
  • Thoughtseize into Hymn or Goyf against known combo, provided the mana is secure.
  • Decay plus Strix/Goyf against Delver when the land sequence can absorb Wasteland.

Common mulligans

  • One-land hands without Deathrite or Brainstorm plus a credible shuffle.
  • Hands whose only mana source is a Deathrite with no land likely to reach a graveyard.
  • Four-mana-heavy hands against tempo.
  • Fair-card hands against combo with no discard, Force, or fast Goyf.
  • Multiple Visions without early interaction when the opponent can win before suspend resolves.

OpponentPreferred developmentCentral question

6 · Key interactions

Cascade turns deck construction into a rules engine

Shardless Agent and Ancestral Vision

Cascade triggers when Agent is cast, before the creature resolves. It exiles cards until it finds a nonland card with mana value less than three. Vision has mana value zero, and cascade allows it to be cast without paying its nonexistent mana cost.

Countering Agent does not stop cascade

Once Agent has been cast, its cascade trigger exists independently. Force of Will on Agent stops the 2/2 but not the cascade spell. Stifle can counter the cascade trigger itself.

Brainstorm and Jace stage the cascade

Put Ancestral Vision from hand on top, then cast Agent. Cascade encounters Vision first. Brainstorm also turns excess Visions, discard, or expensive cards into future draw steps.

A cascade is not always usable

You may cast the exiled card; you are not required to. If Agent finds Abrupt Decay and there is no legal target, it cannot be cast. The unused card and the other exiled cards go randomly to the bottom.

Chalice and Counterbalance see Vision as zero

Casting Vision through cascade still casts a mana-value-zero spell. Chalice at zero counters it. Counterbalance can counter it by revealing a land, because lands have mana value zero.

Suspending Vision

When the final time counter is removed, a trigger instructs its owner to cast Vision. Stifling that trigger leaves Vision exiled with no counters and no ordinary way to cast it later.

Deathrite Shaman’s multiple jobs

Its mana ability can exile a land from either graveyard. Its other abilities attack Snapcaster, threshold, Punishing Fire, Loam, Past in Flames, and Reanimator while controlling life totals.

Baleful Strix changes Tarmogoyf

Strix is both artifact and creature. Once it reaches a graveyard, it may add two card types to Goyf’s calculation. It also replaces itself before trading with nearly any attacker.

Liliana versus True-Name and giant creatures

Liliana’s sacrifice ability does not target. If True-Name, Emrakul, or another protected creature is the opponent’s only creature, it can be removed.

Abrupt Decay and Counterbalance

Decay cannot be countered by spells or abilities and targets a nonland permanent with mana value three or less. It is the premier answer to Counterbalance, Vial, equipment, Delver threats, Rest in Peace, and many prison pieces.

Maelstrom Pulse and tokens

Pulse destroys the target and every other nonland permanent with the same name. Multiple Angel, Goblin, or Zombie tokens often share a name. Pulse still targets, so it cannot remove True-Name.

Golgari Charm’s modes

The -1/-1 mode sweeps True-Name, Elves, Death & Taxes creatures, Goblin tokens, and Pyromancer armies. The enchantment mode answers Blood Moon only if green mana remains available. Regeneration does not stop Terminus, exile, sacrifice, or toughness reduction.

Wasteland is utility removal

Unlike Delver, Shardless wants three and four mana. Wasteland is primarily for Maze, Tar Pit, Grove, Dark Depths, Cloudpost, or a genuinely vulnerable color—not a mandatory activation.

7 · How to pilot the deck

Win the resource war without forgetting the game can end

Shardless is built to win long games, but “long” is not the same as “slow.” Use mana every turn, place the opponent under a clock, and force their answers to line up poorly.

1. Map the mana before fetching

Turn-one black, turn-two double black, turn-three green-blue, and turn-four double blue can all matter. Fetch for the next several turns while accounting for Wasteland, Blood Moon, Port, and the possibility Deathrite dies.

2. Spend mana proactively

If the choice is Agent or Goyf on an empty board, Agent may be correct because a future Wasteland could remove the third mana. The deck often wins by deploying value before tempo can constrict mana.

3. Separate card advantage from survival

Suspending Vision is powerful, but it does not answer Delver, Thalia, Show and Tell, or a lethal Storm turn. Spend a card inefficiently when necessary to reach the turn where Vision matters.

4. Use Force selectively

Force is poor in ordinary attrition but necessary against Blood Moon, Natural Order, Counterbalance in certain hands, combo, and four-mana permanents the maindeck cannot answer.

5. Discard the card your answers cannot cover

Thoughtseize should take the spell that invalidates the existing hand, not simply the most expensive card. Hymn is strongest before Brainstorm can protect key cards and when Goyf or Liliana capitalizes on an empty hand.

6. Protect planeswalkers with disposable value

Agent and Strix have already generated value. Trade their bodies to preserve Jace or Liliana, which can convert a small advantage into a lock.

7. Know when Deathrite is the win condition

Repeated drains ignore blockers and planeswalker combat. Preserve creatures in graveyards and calculate the drain clock before exposing Shaman.

8. Lower the curve against tempo

Bring in Disfigure and Strix while removing some Jaces, discard, or slow cards. Become hellbent through productive casts rather than holding value cards while Daze and Wasteland dictate the game.

9. End combo games

Thoughtseize into Hymn into Liliana is not enough if the opponent gets unlimited draw steps. Deploy Goyf, activate Tar Pit, and drain with Deathrite.

8 · Sideboarding principles

Change which half of the deck matters

Force and discard have different jobs

Force is best against speed, must-answer permanents, and games where card advantage is irrelevant. Discard is best before combo, planeswalkers, sweepers, or equipment can be protected. In topdeck games, both decay.

Ancestral Vision is not untouchable

Vision is exceptional against fair decks and control. Against fast combo it may be too slow. Do not remove it merely because the matchup is aggressive; drawing three often wins if cheap sideboard cards buy time.

Preserve enough blue cards

When Force remains, count Agent, Strix, Brainstorm, Vision, and Jace after sideboarding. Replacing too many blue cards with black and green answers creates Forces that cannot be cast.

Prefer flexible cards

Golgari Charm, Needle, Spellbomb, Seal, Pulse, and Deluge each cover several matchups. The deck sees many cards, so one- and two-copy answers matter.

10 · Historical notes and sources

Why Shardless BUG belongs at the center of the era

Shardless BUG was Legacy’s answer to a format dominated by efficient one-for-one spells. It made Swords, Bolt, Decay, Force, and discard look insufficient by attaching extra cards to creatures and delayed draw engines. It pressured planeswalkers, answered Counterbalance without fighting on the stack, and forced Delver decks to win before the first Vision resolved.

Its weakness was equally defining: it was fair. Cascade excluded ordinary cheap counterspells from the maindeck, its clock was slower than Delver or combo, and its three-color mana was exposed to Stifle, Wasteland, Blood Moon, Port, and Choke. The archetype rewarded metagame judgment as much as technical play.

My final verdict

With perfect hindsight, I would begin with Gerry Thompson’s September 2014 architecture and restore the fourth Ancestral Vision. The reason to register Shardless rather than BUG Delver is that Agent and Vision provide a uniquely powerful long-game engine. The singleton Ponder improves consistency, but it dilutes that identity and is a mediocre cascade relative to the fourth draw-three.

I would retain three Force of Will and four total discard spells game one. Abandoning combo entirely squanders one of BUG’s advantages: unlike Jund, it can interact on the stack. The sideboard’s Flusterstorm is the one hindsight addition.
