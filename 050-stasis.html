The Turn Never Came Back

I · The Pause

The Turn Never Came Back

Stasis does not tap a permanent, destroy a resource, or limit the number of cards a player may use. It removes the untap step itself. The lands, creatures, and artifacts already turned sideways remain exactly as they are, and the ordinary promise that a new turn restores them simply disappears.

For two mana, Alpha allowed one player to suspend the most basic rhythm in Magic. Tap a land. Cast a spell. Begin again. Under Stasis, every tap becomes a permanent decision until the enchantment leaves or another effect intervenes.

The controller is trapped too. Each upkeep demands another blue mana even though the Islands paying that cost do not untap. Every successful Stasis deck is therefore an engineering problem: how to keep time stopped for the opponent while creating a private mechanism that still moves.

The original object

Stasis

{1}{U} · Enchantment

Players skip their untap steps.

At the beginning of your upkeep, sacrifice this enchantment unless you pay {U}.

II · The Rule

Alpha’s absolute rule

Many early prison cards tax one action or constrain one permanent type. Meekstone restrains large creatures after they tap. Winter Orb limits land recovery. Kismet delays newly played permanents.

Stasis is broader. It does not care why a permanent is tapped, what type it has, who controls it, or how much it cost. If the permanent normally relies on the untap step, the permanent stops functioning.

The price is equally broad. The controller receives no exemption. Alpha did not present a finished combo; it presented a symmetrical rule and challenged players to discover asymmetry elsewhere.

Stasis did not freeze the battlefield once. It removed the moment when the battlefield was supposed to recover.The untap step ceased to exist
III · The Procedure

What Stasis actually does

While Stasis remains on the battlefield, every player skips their untap step. A skipped step does not begin, no turn-based untapping occurs, and abilities that trigger at the beginning of that step do not trigger.

Stasis has a separate triggered ability at the beginning of its controller’s upkeep. When that ability resolves, the controller pays one blue mana or sacrifices Stasis.

Multiple copies are strategically redundant but financially demanding. Each Stasis creates its own upkeep trigger, so a player controlling two must pay for each one they wish to keep.

The upkeep still happens. Stasis removes only the untap step. Upkeep triggers, draw steps, main phases, combat, and end steps continue normally unless another effect changes them.

IV · The Missing Step

Skipping the step changes more than untapping

Because the untap step never begins, permanents do not phase in or out through the normal turn procedure. Effects that wait specifically for the untap step also wait indefinitely.

This distinction matters whenever a card says “during your untap step,” “at the beginning of your untap step,” or depends on the ordinary phasing turn action. Stasis does not prevent those events after they occur; it prevents the step containing them from existing.

Removing Stasis during upkeep is too late to recover that turn. The untap step has already been skipped. The player must wait until the next turn unless another effect untaps the relevant permanents.

V · The Exception

Untapping outside the untap step

Stasis does not say that permanents cannot untap. It says players skip their untap steps.

Ley Druid, Twiddle, Ral Zarek, Seedborn Muse, and countless activated or triggered abilities can untap permanents at other times. A land untapped during upkeep can immediately produce the blue mana needed to preserve Stasis.

This is the central design space of the archetype. The opponent depends on the universal turn structure. The Stasis player replaces that structure with card-specific untap effects, bounce, alternative costs, or turn skipping.

VI · The Payment

The blue mana that must already exist

The Stasis controller reaches upkeep with no ordinary untap step behind them. To pay the trigger, an untapped blue source must already be available, a new source must untap through another effect, or mana must be produced without tapping a frozen permanent.

Untapped Island

One more upkeep

The land pays once and remains tapped afterward unless the lock is released.

Outside untap

Ley Druid or Forsaken City

A card-specific effect restores a blue source after the missing untap step.

Remove Stasis

Reset on the next turn

Bounce or destroy the enchantment before the controller’s turn so a normal untap step can occur.

Maintaining Stasis is therefore a countdown. Every blue source represents a number of additional frozen turns unless the deck contains a renewable engine.

VII · The Expenditure

Every new land works once

A land played after Stasis resolves enters untapped unless another effect says otherwise. It can be tapped for mana normally and then remains tapped through later turns.

This makes each land drop a temporary resource. The controller can pay one more upkeep. The opponent can cast one more answer. Neither player receives the land back through the normal turn cycle.

Kismet and Root Maze are so powerful because they remove even that first use. A land that enters tapped beneath Stasis may never produce mana at all.

VIII · The Timing

Destroy or bounce it before your turn

The classic Stasis player does not always maintain the enchantment forever. Instead, the deck removes Stasis during the opponent’s end step.

The controller begins the next turn without Stasis on the battlefield, receives a normal untap step, develops resources, and casts the enchantment again after using those resources.

Boomerang, Chain of Vapor, Time Elemental, and similar effects turn Stasis from a permanent commitment into a repeating one-sided pause.

IX · The Future

Kismet closes the future

Kismet makes artifacts, creatures, and lands controlled by opponents enter tapped. Stasis prevents the untap step that would normally restore them.

The combination addresses the lock’s natural weakness. Without Kismet, an opponent can play a fresh land and spend it immediately on Disenchant, Chaos Orb, or another escape. With Kismet, the answer’s mana source arrives already unusable.

Zak Dolan used both cards in the first World Championship-winning deck. Turbo Stasis later expanded the package to multiple copies because Kismet transformed a temporary freeze into a hard lock on future development.

X · The Acceleration

Howling Mine accelerates the prison

Howling Mine gives each player an additional card during their draw step while it remains untapped. Under Stasis, an untapped Mine stays untapped because there is no untap step in which its status changes.

The extra cards help the Stasis player find Islands, bounce spells, Kismet, countermagic, and another copy of the enchantment. The opponent also draws more cards—but cannot necessarily generate enough fresh mana to cast them.

This is why the archetype became “Turbo Stasis.” The deck accelerates both libraries while constructing a game in which only one player can convert cards into actions.

XI · The Hand

Black Vise turns waiting into damage

Howling Mine causes the opponent’s hand to grow. Stasis prevents lands and creatures from recovering. Black Vise converts the accumulated cards into damage during the opponent’s upkeep.

The opponent faces a contradiction. Casting spells reduces Vise damage but consumes the few untapped mana sources that remain. Holding spells preserves possible answers but increases the Vise clock.

Later lists sometimes win through decking or Brain Freeze instead, but Black Vise remains the iconic finish because it makes the visible symptom of the lock—the overfilled hand—become lethal.

XII · The Champion

Stasis in the first World Champion’s deck

Zak Dolan’s 1994 World Championship deck contained two Stases and one Kismet inside an eclectic four-color control-prison shell.

Ley Druid could untap a land outside the untap step. Time Elemental could return Stasis or another permanent to hand. Serra Angel attacked without tapping. Icy Manipulator, Winter Orb, Meekstone, removal, and counterspells restricted whichever axis remained available.

Stasis was not yet a self-contained archetype. It was one rule among many, chosen because Dolan’s creatures and utility effects were better prepared to function in a world without untapping.

XIII · The Metagame

Turbo Stasis during Black Summer

Necropotence dominated the 1996 Standard season, earning the period the name “Black Summer.” Turbo Stasis became one of the format’s distinctive counterstrategies.

Necropotence could fill its controller’s hand, but that strength became awkward when lands remained tapped and Black Vise punished the resulting card count. Stasis also attacked creature combat without needing to destroy black creatures individually.

Matt Place reached the Top 8 of the 1996 U.S. Nationals with Turbo Stasis, and Tommi Hovi carried the archetype into the quarterfinals of the World Championship later that year.

XIV · The Quarterfinal

Tommi Hovi’s Worlds quarterfinal

Hovi’s deck played four Stases, four Howling Mines, two Kismets, three Despotic Scepters, four Boomerangs, four Force of Wills, and four Lim-Dûl’s Vaults.

The list could find the lock, defend it, and decide exactly when it should end. Land Tax and the Mine supplied cards. Zuran Orb converted excess lands into life. Soldevi Digger and Recall extended the deck’s ability to survive long games.

The deck’s tournament result established Turbo Stasis as a genuine premier-event archetype rather than an elaborate casual lock.

XV · The Lever

Despotic Scepter as an escape hatch

Despotic Scepter can tap to destroy a permanent its controller owns. In Turbo Stasis, that permanent is often Stasis itself.

The Scepter is deployed before the lock. During the opponent’s end step, it destroys Stasis. The controller untaps normally, uses the refreshed mana, and casts another Stasis when ready.

The artifact is narrower than Boomerang but renewable. It turns the enchantment’s sacrifice into a controlled release rather than a failure to pay upkeep.

XVI · The Missing Player

Chronatog skips the upkeep itself

Chronatog allows its controller to skip their next turn. If that player never begins a turn, they never reach the upkeep that demands blue mana for Stasis.

In a two-player game, the controller can activate Chronatog during the opponent’s turn, skip the next turn, wait through another opposing turn, and repeat. The opponent continues drawing cards while receiving no untap steps.

This creates the purest Stasis endgame: the controller stops participating in turn order, and the opponent eventually loses by attempting to draw from an empty library.

XVII · The City

Forsaken City pays through the freeze

Forsaken City does not normally untap during its controller’s untap step. Instead, its controller may exile a card from hand during upkeep to untap it.

That drawback becomes an advantage under Stasis. The missing untap step does not matter because Forsaken City uses its own upkeep trigger. It untaps, produces blue mana, and pays for the enchantment.

Howling Mine, Gush, and other draw engines provide the cards that the City exiles. The lock converts cards in hand directly into additional frozen turns.

XVIII · The Islands

Gush, Daze, and Thwart recycle Islands

Premodern Stasis decks exploit alternate costs that return Islands to hand. Gush draws cards by returning two Islands. Daze and Thwart protect the lock by returning Islands instead of spending mana.

The returned Islands are no longer trapped. They can be replayed untapped on later turns, used once, and returned again by another spell.

What looks like a cost in an ordinary deck becomes resource renewal. The Stasis player repeatedly converts tapped permanents into cards in hand and cards in hand into fresh land drops.

XIX · The Entrance

Root Maze creates the modern hard lock

Root Maze makes artifacts and lands enter tapped for every player. Combined with Stasis, newly played lands never receive their first activation.

The symmetry is broken through Forsaken City, alternative-cost spells, existing untapped Islands, or sequencing Root Maze before the controller has committed to the complete lock.

Root Maze performs Kismet’s most important job for one mana, although it lacks Kismet’s one-sided wording and does not tap creatures. Premodern decks choose between them according to speed, color access, and how completely they can exploit the symmetry.

XX · The Survival

Premodern keeps the archetype alive

Premodern’s card pool ends before Modern begins but includes Forsaken City, Gush, Daze, Thwart, Chain of Vapor, Black Vise, and the original enchantment.

In May 2026, Jacob Lee finished 9–5 at the 326-player North American Premodern Championship with a mono-blue Stasis list splashing red sideboard mana through Shivan Reef.

The result shows that the archetype remains competitively coherent three decades after Black Summer. Its modern historical home is not Legacy, where the environment is faster and more flexible, but a format whose card pool preserves the exact engines that learned to live without untap steps.

XXI · The Ending

How a Stasis deck actually wins

Black Vise

The growing hand becomes damage

Howling Mine accelerates the draw count while frozen mana prevents the opponent from emptying the hand.

Decking

The opponent keeps taking turns

Chronatog, Feldon’s Cane, Soldevi Digger, or recursion lets the controller survive while the opponent draws through the library.

Small attackers or alternate wins

Combat is optional

Vigilance, untap effects, planeswalkers, mill spells, and noncombat damage can end the game without releasing the lock.

A Stasis deck that cannot close the game is merely delaying it. The most successful versions build the win condition into the same engine that maintains the prison.

XXII · The Motion

How opponents break the lock

The most direct answer is to remove Stasis while enough mana remains. Disenchant, Chaos Orb, Chain of Vapor, Force of Vigor, Boseiju, Who Endures, and numerous modern effects can reopen the next untap step.

Mana rocks, creatures that produce mana, alternate costs, untap abilities, and free spells operate without relying on ordinary land recovery. Fresh lands also work once unless Kismet or Root Maze prevents their first use.

The timing matters. Removing Stasis during the controller’s upkeep does not restore the untap step already skipped that turn. Removing it during the previous end step does.

XXIII · The Table

The multiplayer social contract

In Commander, one Stasis removes the untap step from every player at the table. The controller must still pay only one blue mana each upkeep, making the effect scale aggressively with additional opponents.

Commanders and engines that untap permanents outside the untap step, produce mana without tapping, or operate from artifacts can make the lock one-sided. The card is legal, but the practical expectation is clear: a deck casting it should have a demonstrated route to victory.

Without a clock, Stasis may convert a multiplayer game into several people drawing one card and passing. The card’s strategic legitimacy and its social reputation are therefore inseparable.

XXIV · The Bloodline

The descendants of frozen time

Winter Orb limits land untapping rather than deleting the entire step. Static Orb restricts each player to two permanents. Rising Waters allows only one land to untap.

The artifact descendant

Static Orb

Static Orb preserves the universal prison but leaves each player two untaps. It is easier to exploit with tapping effects and less absolute than removing the step itself.

Frozen Aether and Kismet attack permanents as they enter. Back to Basics prevents nonbasic lands from untapping. Vorinclex, Voice of Hunger combines acceleration with selective land suppression.

Later designs generally narrow the affected permanents, provide an untap allowance, raise the mana cost, or attach the effect to a more vulnerable permanent. Stasis remains the cleanest and harshest statement: no untap steps.

XXV · The Image

Fay Jones’s single Magic painting

Seattle artist Fay Jones created only one illustration for Magic, and it became one of the game’s most recognizable visual departures.

The figures, colors, and flattened space do not resemble the dragons, wizards, weapons, or landscapes surrounding Stasis in Alpha. The art feels like a self-contained painting that happens to occupy a card frame.

That difference became part of the card’s identity. The original image remained through Fifth Edition and returned in the 30th Anniversary Edition. In 2022, Jérémie Solomon created a new borderless interpretation for the Secret Lair drop The Space Beyond the Stars, presenting stasis as vast cosmic suspension rather than intimate abstraction.

XXVI · The Habitats

A life across formats

Historic Standard and Old School

World Champion and Black Summer prison

Zak Dolan used it in 1994; Turbo Stasis became a premier Standard archetype in 1996.

Premodern

The complete Forsaken City engine

Gush, Daze, Thwart, Chain of Vapor, Black Vise, and Forsaken City preserve a dedicated competitive archetype.

Legacy, Vintage, and Commander

Legal but specialized

The card remains available in eternal formats, where speed, free interaction, and social expectations limit its ordinary use.

Stasis is currently legal in Legacy, Vintage, Commander, and Oathbreaker. It is not legal in Standard, Pioneer, Modern, Pauper, Historic, Timeless, Brawl, or current digital rotating formats.

XXVII · The Feeling

What players remember

The opponent said “untap,” looked at the enchantment, and moved directly to upkeep.
Stasis made the most automatic action in Magic become the action nobody was allowed to take.

Players remember turning lands sideways and arranging them into a permanent record of every spell cast after the lock. They remember counting Islands, floating blue mana, and deciding whether the final untapped source should preserve Stasis or answer something else.

They remember the silence of a board that continued acquiring cards without recovering motion. The game still had turns. It simply no longer had beginnings.

XXIX · The Object

Printings and sources

Stasis appeared in every core set from Alpha through Fifth Edition with Fay Jones’s original artwork. It then went more than two decades without a new paper printing. The 30th Anniversary Edition reproduced the original card as a non-tournament-legal facsimile, while the 2022 Secret Lair drop The Space Beyond the Stars introduced the first new artwork and the first traditional foil paper version.
