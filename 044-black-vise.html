I · The Compression

The Opening Hand Was Already Burning

Black Vise is a one-mana artifact that can deal three damage before its chosen opponent has cast a spell. It does not attack, target a permanent, or require another card. It simply looks at the opening hand and declares that possessing options has become painful.

The card reverses one of Magic's most basic comforts. A full hand normally means safety: lands, threats, answers, and several possible futures. Black Vise turns those possibilities into a second life total. Every card above four becomes another point of pressure, and every turn spent planning becomes another upkeep spent inside the machine.

Its greatest decks did not wait for the opponent to empty the hand naturally. They supplied more cards with Howling Mine, removed mana with Winter Orb and Strip Mine, and destroyed lands with Armageddon or Stone Rain. The opponent was not punished for refusing to play. The opponent was prevented from playing, then punished for the evidence.

II · The Instrument

Alpha’s one-mana punishment

Alpha contained several artifacts that transformed abstract game resources into pressure. The Rack punished an empty hand. Iron Star and its cycle converted colored spells into life. Meekstone restrained large creatures. Black Vise attacked the opposite end of the hand-size spectrum from The Rack.

At one generic mana, the artifact could enter almost any deck and begin operating before colored strategy mattered. A red deck, white prison deck, blue artifact deck, or multicolor control shell could all make the same opening play.

The name says “black,” but the card has no black color identity. The adjective describes the instrument, not the mana required to use it. That distinction helped make Black Vise one of the earliest colorless cards to influence how every deck thought about its opening hand.

Black Vise made the strongest-looking resource in the game—seven cards in hand—begin as a liability.Abundance converted directly into damage
III · The Number

Why seven cards meant three damage

A normal opening hand contains seven cards. Black Vise ignores the first four and deals damage equal to the rest. Seven cards therefore produce three damage; six produce two; five produce one; four or fewer produce none.

Seven cards

Three damage

A turn-one Vise begins as a Lightning Bolt that threatens to repeat if the opponent cannot empty the hand.

Five or six

Continuing pressure

The artifact turns every delayed land or expensive answer into another point during the next upkeep.

Four or fewer

The machine goes quiet

The ability still triggers, but the calculation produces no positive damage.

This creates an immediate race against one's own cards. The opponent must decide whether to cast inefficiently, expose important spells, or accept the damage while preserving a better sequence.

IV · The Victim

Choosing the victim

Modern Oracle text instructs Black Vise's controller to choose an opponent as the artifact enters the battlefield. The choice is not a target and does not use the stack. Once made, it remains attached to that particular opponent.

In a duel the choice is automatic. In multiplayer it is strategic and political. The chosen player may have the fullest hand, the slowest deck, or simply the position the controller most wants to pressure.

If that player leaves the game, Black Vise remains on the battlefield but has no new victim. It does not automatically choose another opponent. A copied Vise entering the battlefield makes its own independent choice.

V · The Trigger

What Black Vise actually does

At the beginning of the chosen player's upkeep, Black Vise triggers. When the ability resolves, the game counts the cards currently in that player's hand, subtracts four, and deals that much damage if the result is positive.

The hand is checked on resolution, not when the trigger is created. The chosen player may respond by casting instants, activating abilities that discard cards, or otherwise reducing the hand before damage is calculated. Effects that add cards before resolution can increase the damage.

Destroying Black Vise after its ability has triggered does not remove that trigger from the stack. The game uses the source's last known information as necessary and still counts the chosen player's hand when the ability resolves.

No negative damage. Four or fewer cards produce zero damage. Black Vise never gains life or reverses direction when the calculation falls below zero.

VI · The Resource

The hand as a life total

Black Vise asks players to manage hand size as carefully as life. Drawing an extra card may cost damage. Holding a reactive spell may cost damage. Missing a land drop is painful twice: the player cannot cast the cards already held, and the Vise punishes those cards for remaining.

The artifact also changes card valuation. A cheap spell gains hidden value because it can escape the hand. A powerful six-mana card may become a liability if the mana base is under attack. Card advantage is no longer automatically advantageous when the additional cards cannot be converted into action.

This is the inverse of The Rack. The Rack rewards discard and attrition by punishing scarcity. Black Vise rewards mana denial and forced drawing by punishing abundance.

VII · The Gift

Howling Mine fills the trap

Howling Mine appears to help the opponent escape by providing more options. Black Vise reveals the opposite possibility. The Mine places cards into the hand faster than the opponent may be able to spend them.

The combination is not automatically successful. If the opponent has abundant mana and cheap spells, extra cards can allow the hand to shrink even faster. The surrounding deck must restrict the ability to deploy those cards.

In Vise-Orb control, Howling Mine supplied the raw material while Winter Orb, Icy Manipulator, Strip Mine, and Armageddon controlled the rate of spending. The “gift” and the punishment were parts of the same machine.

VIII · The Lock

Winter Orb prevents the escape

Winter Orb limits the lands each player can untap while it remains untapped. That restriction keeps spells trapped in hand and allows Black Vise to continue dealing damage long after a normal opponent would have played below four cards.

Icy Manipulator makes the relationship asymmetric. It can tap Winter Orb before its controller's untap step, allowing that player to recover all lands, then leave the Orb untapped for the opponent. It can also tap the opponent's one available land during upkeep.

The Vise supplies the clock that a prison deck otherwise lacks. The lock does not need a creature or a large finishing spell. Every upkeep spent holding unusable cards advances the victory condition.

IX · The Ruin

Armageddon leaves the cards stranded

Armageddon appears dangerous beside Black Vise because both players lose their lands. The prison deck prepares for the symmetry through artifact mana, Land Tax, Zuran Orb, low costs, and a board already designed around the coming destruction.

The opponent may retain a large hand filled with spells that were intended for later turns. Once the lands disappear, those cards become the source of recurring Vise damage.

Black Vise changes Armageddon from pure resource denial into a measurable clock. The destroyed lands explain why the hand cannot shrink; the Vise ensures that inability ends the game.

X · The Championship

The Vise at the 1995 World Championship

The 1995 World Championship demonstrated Black Vise at both strategic extremes. Marc Hernandez reached the final with four copies in a white-red Vise-Orb control deck. Andrea Redi reached the quarterfinals with four copies in mono-red.

Hernandez used Vise as the endpoint of a prison: Howling Mine increased hand size, Winter Orb and Icy Manipulator controlled mana, Island Sanctuary and removal protected life, and Armageddon closed the escape route.

Redi combined the same artifact with Stone Rain, Strip Mine, burn, and Nevinyrral's Disk. The deck could deal early damage, attack mana, then reset the battlefield while the Vise continued watching the hand.

XI · The Curve

The Vise inside Sligh

Jay Schneider's original Geeba deck, made famous by Paul Sligh's 1996 PTQ finish, used one Black Vise. The list's larger contribution was the mathematical mana curve, but the Vise fit that theory perfectly.

It cost one mana, used the first turn efficiently, and demanded no future investment. The opponent could empty the hand to silence it, but doing so meant deploying creatures and artifacts into Incinerate, Lightning Bolt, Orcish Artillery, and battlefield pressure.

In aggressive red, Black Vise was less a prison win condition than a conditional burn spell. Three early damage was enough; the deck did not require the artifact to remain active forever.

XII · The Failure

The dead topdeck problem

Black Vise is strongest in the opening hand and weakest after both players have spent their resources. A late Vise against an opponent holding two cards may do nothing for the remainder of the game.

It also fails against decks built from cheap spells. An opponent who can deploy several cards immediately turns the artifact off without sacrificing tempo. Fast creature decks may welcome a one-mana play that does not affect combat.

This polarity explains the card's historical danger and modern decline. In the right opening it resembles extraordinary burn. In the wrong draw step it is blank cardboard. Formats became faster and better at emptying hands, reducing the frequency of the first experience.

XIII · The List

Restriction, banning, and fear

Black Vise entered the Standard restricted list in February 1996, alongside cards such as Strip Mine, Land Tax, Hymn to Tourach, and Zuran Orb. Standard eliminated its restricted list in January 1997 by moving restricted cards to the banned list.

Its association with non-games was easy to understand. Multiple turn-one Vises could deal enormous damage before the opponent had meaningful choices, while resource-denial decks prolonged that damage by preventing normal development.

Because the old Type 1.5 banned cards restricted in Type I, Black Vise spent years outside the format that eventually became Legacy. Its reputation survived longer than its competitive evidence.

XIV · The Return

The 2015 Legacy unbanning

Wizards unbanned Black Vise in Legacy in September 2015. The announcement acknowledged that the artifact had historically been powerful against card-drawing decks and, at times, powerful enough to limit metagames.

R&D concluded that modern Legacy was strong enough to absorb it and hoped the card might pressure blue draw strategies. The feared renaissance did not occur. Delver decks, combo decks, free interaction, cheap cantrips, and efficient threats could empty or manipulate hands too effectively.

The unbanning became a historical verdict. Black Vise's old play pattern was once dangerous enough to prohibit; the modern format had evolved beyond the conditions that made it universal.

XV · The Table

One chosen opponent

In Commander, Black Vise targets the politics of hand size as much as the arithmetic. The controller chooses one opponent when the artifact enters, publicly identifying the player expected to suffer.

A seven-card hand still means three damage, but forty life and multiple opponents reduce the importance of that opening burst. The card becomes more interesting beside forced draw, bounce, prison pieces, or commanders that punish large hands.

The inability to choose a new opponent when the first leaves the game limits its multiplayer reach. Unlike a global punishment, the Vise is a personal commitment.

XVI · The Bloodline

The descendants of Black Vise

Iron Maiden raises the casting cost to three and moves the safe threshold from four cards to four in essentially the same upkeep calculation. Viseling attaches a similar effect to a creature, making it easier to remove through combat and creature spells.

The fixed-threshold descendant

Ebony Owl Netsuke

Ebony Owl Netsuke deals four damage when the opponent begins upkeep with seven or more cards. It became the namesake finisher of Owling Mine, translating Black Vise's variable pressure into a larger fixed punishment.

Fevered Visions combines extra drawing with damage for large hands. Sudden Impact and Gaze of Adamaro convert hand size into a single burst. The Rack mirrors the original from the other direction.

Later designs usually increase the cost, raise the threshold, attach the effect to a more vulnerable permanent, or require a second color. Black Vise remains exceptional because the punishment begins for one generic mana.

XVII · The Image

The instrument and its victim

Richard Thomas's original artwork places a human face inside a dark mechanical vise. The composition is immediate and uncomfortable: gold handles, black jaws, closed eyes, and a mouth forced into silence by pressure.

The same artwork followed the card through twelve early printings, becoming inseparable from its identity. Dan Murayama Scott's From the Vault: Relics version finally reimagined it in 2010, replacing the restrained device with a more elaborate magical instrument and visible energy.

Mystery Booster 2 returned the newer artwork in a Future Sight frame, turning a punishment from Alpha into an object presented as though it belonged to an alternate future.

XVIII · The Habitats

A life across formats

Old School

A defining opening threat

Rulesets commonly restrict Black Vise because multiple copies can produce extreme first-turn damage alongside artifact mana and denial.

Legacy and Vintage

Legal, rarely central

Legacy has allowed four copies since 2015, while current Vintage does not restrict it; neither format treats it as a universal staple.

Commander

A political single-target clock

It is legal and most effective in forced-draw or hand-size-punishment decks rather than as an isolated turn-one play.

Black Vise is not Modern, Pioneer, or Pauper legal. Its later premium printings did not place it into a Modern-legal expansion, and it has no qualifying common printing.

XIX · The Feeling

What players remember

The game began with seven cards. The Vise counted three of them as damage.
Black Vise made the opening hand feel less like preparation and more like something already trapped inside a machine.

Players remember laying it down before a land. They remember multiple copies and counting the same hand several times. They remember discarding or casting cards badly simply to fall beneath five before the next upkeep.

They remember the paradox of Howling Mine: drawing another card while hoping to escape a card that punished drawing. Most of all, they remember when one generic mana was enough to make an entire control deck feel late.

XXI · The Object

Printings and sources

Black Vise retained Richard Thomas's original artwork through every early tournament-legal printing, ending with Fourth Edition. From the Vault: Relics introduced the first new art in 2010, by Dan Murayama Scott. Mystery Booster 2 reused that interpretation in a Future Sight frame in 2024.
