Legacy archetype dossier · 2012–September 2014

Death & Taxes

Legacy’s creature prison deck: every harmless-looking white creature closes a door, every land constricts the opponent’s mana, and Aether Vial turns the rules of timing into a weapon.

Historical scope. This guide follows Death & Taxes from the printing of Thalia, Guardian of Thraben in early 2012 through September 2014, immediately before Treasure Cruise reshaped Legacy. The deck existed earlier, but Thalia completed the recognizable Vial–Port–Wasteland architecture documented here.
1 · Cover and deck identity

A control deck disguised as white creatures

Death & Taxes is often mistaken for White Weenie because nearly all of its spells are creatures and it eventually wins through combat. Its actual identity is closer to a prison or resource-denial deck. Thalia increases spell costs. Rishadan Port and Wasteland restrict mana. Phyrexian Revoker disables engines. Mother of Runes protects whichever permanent is currently holding the lock together. Aether Vial lets the deck deploy these effects without spending mana or using the stack normally.

12 cards
The mana prison.

Four Thalia, four Ports, and four Wastelands make normal Legacy curves operate a turn late.

4 Vials
Break the symmetry.

Your creatures enter without paying Thalia’s tax, through countermagic, and at tactically devastating times.

1 weak link
That is all D&T needs.

The deck identifies a dependency—mana, activated ability, graveyard, equipment, or search—and attacks it repeatedly.

Why the name fits

The joke is that death and taxes are unavoidable. The strategy likewise imposes small, cumulative costs that cannot be ignored. No individual Port activation, Thalia tax, Revoker, or Flickerwisp trick necessarily ends the game. Together they create a position where the opponent possesses powerful cards but cannot use them in the required sequence.

2 · What this deck does

Build a prison one ordinary permanent at a time

D&T rarely locks an opponent out with a single permanent. It assembles overlapping inconveniences. A spell costs one more, a land is tapped during upkeep, another land is destroyed, the key activated ability is turned off, and removal cannot touch the protected creature. The opponent is forced to spend an entire turn solving one component while the remaining components attack.

Develop without spending mana

Vial lets Ports and Wastelands function as spells while creatures continue to enter. This is the deck’s fundamental resource advantage.

Tax the correct resource

Against Storm it is spell mana; against Delver it may be a color; against Miracles it may be white mana, Top, or the Terminus window.

Convert small creatures into real threats

Stoneforge Mystic and equipment make Mother, Thalia, Flickerwisp, and Avenger capable of ending the game instead of merely prolonging it.

Turn knowledge into virtual cards

Revoker naming the correct card or Flickerwisp hitting the correct permanent can invalidate several opposing cards without drawing anything.

The deck does not answer cards; it attacks systems. The best play is often not removing the threat in front of you, but disabling the mana, ability, or timing structure that allows the opponent’s deck to function.
3 · History and evolution

From cult deck to Grand Prix champion

Before 2012 · The Vial–Mangara lineage

The strange white control deck already exists

Earlier builds used Aether Vial, Port, Karakas, Mangara, Flickerwisp, Serra Avenger, Stoneforge Mystic, and various disruptive white creatures. The deck was respected by specialists but remained a fringe strategy with a poor Storm matchup.

February 2012 · Dark Ascension

Thalia supplies the missing universal tax

Thalia attacked combo, control, cantrip decks, and removal while contributing a first-striking clock. Unlike a normal prison artifact, she could be protected by Mother, bounced by Karakas, and deployed through Vial.

Late 2012–January 2013 · The shell crystallizes

Stoneforge, Revoker, Mangara, and Avengers surround Thalia

The deck still emphasized the repeatable Mangara endgame and played several legendary lands. Rest in Peace joined the sideboard and dramatically improved graveyard interaction.

April 2013 · Grand Prix Strasbourg

Two Danes put D&T into the Top 4

Thomas Enevoldsen won the 1,365-player Grand Prix and Michael Bonde reached the semifinals. The result moved Death & Taxes from specialist curiosity to established Legacy pillar.

November 2013 · Legacy Championship

Ari Lax confirms the result

Lax won the Legacy Championship with essentially the Strasbourg maindeck and an Enlightened Tutor sideboard, proving the core was robust across different pilots and events.

Late 2013–2014 · True-Name Nemesis

The ground becomes hostile

True-Name plus equipment created races ordinary white creatures could not win. Golgari Charm, Zealous Persecution, Dread of Night, and Massacre also became common collateral hate. Lists moved away from Mangara and toward Serra Avenger, Sword of Fire and Ice, and faster aerial pressure.

Born of the Gods–Conspiracy 2014

Spirit and Judgment expand the toolbox

Spirit of the Labyrinth punished Brainstorm and Ponder; Council’s Judgment provided a rare mono-white answer to True-Name and awkward permanents. Leonin Arbiter/Ghost Quarter experiments pushed the search-denial theme further.

September 2014 · The cutoff

The settled pre-Cruise form

The conventional list used Avengers and Flickerwisps to exploit the window created by Thalia, Port, Wasteland, Revoker, and Vial. The extreme Arbiter builds remained metagame tools rather than the default.

Author’s reconstruction

5 · Mulligans and opening turns

Keep engines and sequences, not merely white mana

A D&T hand should describe how the next three turns constrain the opponent. Vial plus Port is a plan. Plains, Thalia, Wasteland, and a second white source is a plan. Four lands and three creatures may be functional but can lack the leverage that makes those creatures competitive Legacy cards.

General keeps

  • Turn-one Vial with two functional lands and creatures distributed between two and three mana.
  • Two white sources plus Thalia, especially against unknown opponents or spell-based combo.
  • Mother into Stoneforge against fair decks, provided the mana survives Wasteland.
  • Port/Wasteland plus Vial when the opponent’s mana is likely to be the weak link.
  • Revoker with enough context against known Top, Deathrite, LED, Sneak Attack, planeswalker, or equipment decks.

Common mulligans

  • Colorless land as the only mana source without Vial.
  • One Plains, Vial, and multiple three-drops when losing Vial leaves the hand inert.
  • Hands with Port and Wasteland but no white source.
  • Slow creature piles against combo with no Thalia, Revoker, Vial, or Canonist plan.
  • Equipment-heavy hands without Stoneforge or enough mana to deploy them.

Vial does not excuse a bad hand. If the hand cannot cast its spells after Vial is countered, Needled, Decayed, or destroyed, the apparent turn-one engine may be a trap.
6 · Key interactions

The rules are part of the card pool

Aether Vial and Thalia

Vial puts a creature onto the battlefield; it does not cast it. This bypasses Thalia’s additional cost and countermagic. Vialing Thalia in after an opponent has already cast a spell does not retroactively increase that spell’s cost—the cost was paid before Thalia entered.

Vial and Serra Avenger

Serra Avenger’s restriction applies only to casting it. Vial may put Avenger onto the battlefield during either player’s turn, including the first three turns of the game.

Mangara and Karakas

Activate Mangara targeting a permanent, retain priority, then return Mangara to your hand with Karakas. Mangara’s ability exists independently on the stack and still exiles the target when it resolves. Vial can redeploy Mangara, creating repeatable removal. The same protection pattern can save Thalia from removal and return her through Vial.

Flickerwisp after the end-step trigger point

If Flickerwisp enters during the opponent’s end step after “beginning of the end step” triggers have already triggered, the exiled permanent does not return until the next end step—usually after an entire turn cycle. This can remove a land, blocker, Counterbalance, equipment, or planeswalker for your full turn.

Flickerwisp and tokens

A token that leaves the battlefield cannot return. Flickerwisp therefore permanently removes Germ, Angel, Goblin, Zombie, or other tokens. Flickering Batterskull itself causes it to return and create a new Germ through living weapon.

Flickerwisp and Aether Vial

Exiling Vial and returning it creates a new permanent with zero counters. This can reset a Vial that has climbed past the part of the curve you need. It can also remove an opposing Vial’s accumulated counters.

Flickerwisp and Oblivion Ring

With the original Oblivion Ring template, allow its exile trigger to go on the stack, then Flickerwisp the Ring before that trigger resolves. The leave-the-battlefield trigger resolves first and returns nothing; the original exile trigger then resolves and exiles its target permanently. The Ring later returns and can exile another permanent.

Stoneforge Mystic and equipment deployment

Stoneforge’s activated ability puts equipment onto the battlefield rather than casting it, bypassing Thalia and countermagic. Batterskull entering this way still triggers living weapon. Activate at the opponent’s end step when possible so mana and information remain available.

Mother of Runes and equipment

Protection from a color can stop removal or make a creature unblockable by creatures of that color. Protection from artifacts, however, causes equipment to become illegally attached and fall off. Choose protection carefully when Jitte or a Sword is carrying the game.

Phyrexian Revoker versus Pithing Needle

Revoker can name nonland cards and shuts down activated abilities including mana abilities; Needle may name lands but explicitly does not stop mana abilities. Revoker can disable Lion’s Eye Diamond, equipment, Deathrite Shaman, planeswalkers, Sensei’s Divining Top, Sneak Attack, Heritage Druid, and many other engines.

Rishadan Port in upkeep

Port a land during upkeep so mana floated in response normally empties before the main phase. Wait for Sensei’s Divining Top activations when appropriate, because the opponent must pass priority before leaving upkeep. Porting the correct color is more important than tapping the most impressive land.

Spirit of the Labyrinth and Brainstorm

If an opponent has already drawn a card that turn, Brainstorm draws no cards but still requires two cards from hand to be placed on top. Vialing Spirit in response to Brainstorm can therefore transform one of Legacy’s best spells into a severe liability.

Aven Mindcensor and Leonin Arbiter are symmetrical

Both interfere with your own Stoneforge Mystic searches. Sequence Stoneforge first or be prepared to pay Arbiter’s tax. Mindcensor also restricts your own search to the top four cards.

7 · How to pilot the deck

Find the weak link, then make every card attack it

Death & Taxes does not have Brainstorm to repair mistakes. Each land activation, Vial counter, Revoker name, and Flickerwisp target must be chosen with the opponent’s future turns in mind.

1. Identify the opponent’s dependency

RUG may depend on green mana. Miracles may depend on Top or double white. Storm depends on casting several noncreature spells in one turn. Elves depends on activated creatures, searching, and a critical-mass turn. Revoker, Port, Wasteland, Thalia, and sideboard cards should converge on the same dependency.

2. Treat lands as spells

Port and Wasteland are not simply mana sources. Before playing a land, decide whether it will cast a spell, deny a color, protect a legend, or constrain a future turn. Activating Wasteland merely because a dual exists can be wrong when the white mana is needed to advance the board.

3. Plan Vial counters two turns ahead

Leaving Vial on two preserves Thalia, Stoneforge, Revoker, Spirit, and Avenger. Moving to three unlocks Flickerwisp, Crusader, Mindcensor, and Mangara. The cards in hand matter, but so does which effects must remain threatening even when they are not actually present.

4. Name the card that enables the deck

Revoker should not automatically name the first activated ability you see. Naming Deathrite may shut off mana and graveyard pressure; naming Jace may protect a developed board; naming Top may dismantle Miracles; naming LED may prevent a Storm kill. Consider the opponent’s hand, mana, and likely sideboard before announcing.

5. Port with a purpose

Porting every upkeep is not mandatory. Sometimes casting Stoneforge or equipping Jitte creates more pressure. Port when it prevents a spell, denies a color, forces action into your open mana, or ensures Thalia’s tax consumes the entire turn.

6. Do not overextend into sweepers

Against Miracles, Terminus ignores Mother and equipment protection. Against BUG, Golgari Charm or Zealous Persecution can undo several turns. Present enough pressure to demand an answer, then retain a Vial creature, Stoneforge, or equipment line for rebuilding.

7. Convert the lock into a clock

A Port lock that never kills gives the opponent draw steps to escape. Equip a flyer, attack with Revoker, or deploy Crusader once the important route is closed. The deck’s prison is soft; the combat step makes it permanent.

8. Preserve priority explicitly

Mangara tricks and certain Flickerwisp sequences require clear priority handling. Announce that you retain priority rather than relying on shortcuts the opponent may interpret differently.

9. Know the opposing deck deeply

D&T’s cards are narrow only when named or timed incorrectly. The deck rewards knowing fetchland counts, basic-land access, equipment packages, combo choke points, and sideboard sweepers across the entire format.

8 · Sideboarding principles

Replace dead creatures without dismantling the machine

Aether Vial usually stays

Vial is the engine that lets utility lands operate as disruption and makes creature timing unpredictable. It can be trimmed only in rare attrition games where the opponent has abundant artifact hate and the match is expected to become pure topdecking. Even then, removing it changes the deck’s mana requirements.

Thalia is matchup-specific despite being iconic

Thalia is essential against combo, Miracles, cantrip decks, and spell-heavy tempo. She is less impressive against creature decks that cast through Vial, Cavern, or mana creatures. In the mirror or against Goblins, the first-strike body may matter more than the tax.

Remove equipment packages by function

Jitte is best in creature combat. Batterskull is best in damage races and attrition. Sword of Fire and Ice is best against blue creatures, True-Name, and slow control. Stoneforge may remain even when one equipment leaves because tutoring and shuffling still carry value.

Do not overload on noncreature spells

Thalia taxes your own Cataclysm, Judgment, Rest in Peace, and removal. Sideboarding twelve spells into the deck can make its own mana denial self-destructive and reduce Vial density. Bring narrow cards only when they attack a central dependency.

10 · Historical notes and sources

Why Death & Taxes belongs in the Golden Era

Death & Taxes demonstrated that Legacy’s power did not require blue cantrips, dual lands, or fast combo. It competed by understanding those cards better than their owners did. Fetchlands created Wasteland and Mindcensor targets. Cheap noncreature spells made Thalia exceptional. Activated engines gave Revoker names. Expensive multicolor mana bases made Port a real spell.

The deck’s difficulty was not mechanical complexity alone. It demanded broad format knowledge. A pilot had to know which card to name, which color to Port, whether Vial belonged on two or three, what a Flickerwisp could invalidate, and when the soft prison had created enough time to attack.

My final verdict

With perfect hindsight, I would not choose the four-Arbiter/four-Ghost-Quarter branch for an open pre-Cruise gauntlet. It had an exceptional Miracles plan but weakened the deck against Delver, Elves, ordinary creature combat, and any game in which the opponent escaped the initial lock.

The mature flying build is the stronger default. Serra Avenger and Flickerwisp solve the True-Name-era battlefield while surviving much of the collateral -1/-1 hate. I would main all three Stoneforge targets because each covers a distinct class of game: Jitte for creatures, Sword of Fire and Ice for blue boards and card advantage, and Batterskull for racing and attrition. Council’s Judgment is the hindsight sideboard upgrade that older Strasbourg lists lacked.
