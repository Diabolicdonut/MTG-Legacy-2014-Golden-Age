The Story That Would Not End

Shahrazad suspended the game on the table, created another Magic game inside it, and turned the literary structure of a story within a story into one of the strangest rules objects ever printed.

I · The Interruption

The Story That Would Not End

Shahrazad does not create a token, change a life total immediately, or place another permanent onto the battlefield. It stops the game being played and asks both players to begin another game using the cards still hidden in their libraries.

The battlefield remains where it is. The hands, graveyards, exiled cards, and spells waiting in the main game are suspended like a scene abandoned at the end of a chapter. The players shuffle the remaining libraries, draw new hands, and begin again inside the unresolved sorcery.

When the second game ends, the loser returns to the first game and loses half of their remaining life. The cards used in the story are gathered and shuffled back into the original libraries. Then the sorcery finally finishes resolving, and the first game continues as though an entire other game had been a single effect on the stack.

The original object

Shahrazad

{W}{W} · Sorcery

Players play a Magic subgame, using their libraries as their decks. Each player who doesn’t win the subgame loses half their life, rounded up.

II · The Expansion

Arabian Nights becomes a game within the game

Arabian Nights was Magic’s first expansion and Richard Garfield’s direct adaptation of stories, people, places, and objects from One Thousand and One Nights. Rather than merely naming familiar figures, the set repeatedly attempted to translate their narrative identities into rules.

Flying Carpet granted flying. Aladdin stole artifacts. Sindbad drew a card and discarded it if the revealed discovery was not land. Shahrazad told a story so compelling that the current reality had to wait for its conclusion.

The result was one of the purest flavor designs ever printed and one of the least practical tournament cards. The mechanic and the literary reference are inseparable: the card works because the story temporarily becomes more important than the world surrounding it.

Shahrazad does not represent a story. It makes the players live inside one before the original game is allowed to continue.Flavor expanded into an entire second match
III · The Tale

Why Shahrazad tells another story

In the frame narrative of One Thousand and One Nights, Shahrazad marries a king who has been executing each new wife. Every night she begins a story and stops at a compelling point before dawn. The king postpones her death because he wants to hear the conclusion.

Stories contain other stories. Characters become narrators, and one unfinished tale opens into another. Survival depends on continuation rather than conclusion.

Shahrazad reproduces that nesting literally. The main game is the king’s chamber. The subgame is the new story. Casting another Shahrazad inside the subgame creates another level, another delay, and another reason the outer game cannot yet end.

IV · The Subgame

What Shahrazad actually does

When Shahrazad resolves, every player in the main game plays a completely separate Magic subgame. The main game is temporarily discontinued while the subgame exists.

The effect uses each player’s main-game library as that player’s subgame deck. The subgame has its own zones, starting procedure, turns, spells, permanents, life totals, and winner or draw result.

Effects in the main game do not ordinarily function inside the subgame, and events in the subgame do not ordinarily trigger abilities waiting in the main game. The two games communicate only where Shahrazad and the dedicated subgame rules explicitly say they do.

A separate game, not a side battle. The subgame follows the normal process for beginning and playing Magic. The original battlefield is not used as the starting battlefield of the new game.
Kaja Foglio’s original ShahrazadThe storyteller sits at the center while listeners gather around her. The image is quiet because her power is not an explosion or weapon; it is the ability to make everyone else remain until the tale is finished.
V · The Separation

Only the libraries enter the subgame

As the subgame begins, each player moves the cards in their main-game library into a newly created subgame library and shuffles them.

Cards in the main-game hand, battlefield, graveyard, exile, command zone, and stack remain in the main game. The players may place those zones aside, record tapped status and counters, and preserve the exact game state for later.

This creates a strategic consequence: the subgame deck contains whatever the main game has not already drawn, cast, milled, discarded, or exiled. The deck is related to the original construction but is no longer the same sixty cards.

VI · The Beginning

A new game begins from the remaining decks

The subgame follows normal starting procedures. A starting player is determined, players draw opening hands from the transferred libraries, and mulligans may be taken under the rules governing the game.

A player whose remaining library cannot support the required opening process may lose immediately. Casting Shahrazad late can therefore make the subgame radically smaller—or transform depleted libraries into a direct danger.

The main game’s life total does not become the subgame’s starting life. The subgame is its own Magic game, and only its final result returns to affect the suspended game.

VII · The Price

Winning half the opponent’s life

After the subgame ends, each player who did not win it loses half their life in the main game, rounded up.

20 life

Lose ten

The first successful Shahrazad can function like a ten-life swing if cast before the main-game life total changes.

An odd total

Round the loss upward

A player at nine loses five and returns at four.

Repeated wins

The loss diminishes

Each additional halving removes less absolute life, but rapidly approaches a total ordinary burn can finish.

The effect causes loss of life rather than damage. Prevention, protection, and effects concerned specifically with damage do not stop it.

VIII · The Nonwinner

What happens when nobody wins

Shahrazad does not say that only the loser loses life. It says each player who does not win loses half.

If the subgame ends in a draw, nobody won. Every participating player therefore loses half their life in the main game.

This wording makes alternate draw conditions especially strange. A deck built around Divine Intervention can intentionally finish the inner story without a winner and return both players to the outer game at reduced life totals.

IX · The Return

How the main game resumes

At the end of the subgame, each player gathers the traditional cards they own in the subgame, places them into their main-game library, and shuffles. This includes cards in the subgame’s exile zone and most cards brought into the subgame from elsewhere.

The temporary subgame zones cease to exist. The main game resumes from the exact point where it stopped, and Shahrazad finishes resolving by applying the life loss.

The spell then enters the main-game graveyard normally. The battlefield, hands, and other recorded zones return unchanged except for effects the subgame rules explicitly carried back.

X · The Depth

A subgame can contain another subgame

Because the subgame library may still contain another copy of Shahrazad, a player can cast it inside the first subgame. The current subgame is suspended and a second subgame begins using the libraries that remained inside the first.

There is no special rule limiting the nesting to one level. Each new copy creates another set of zones, another pair of opening hands, another life total, and another result that must be resolved before the game above it can continue.

The diminishing libraries eventually constrain the depth, but recursion, copies, larger decks, and altered construction can make the theoretical structure enormous. The card's literary nesting becomes a logistical stack of tables, notes, and card piles.

XI · The Echo

Forking the story

Fork and other copy effects can copy Shahrazad while the original spell remains on the stack. The copy resolves first and creates a subgame.

After that subgame ends and the copy finishes resolving, the original Shahrazad is still waiting on the stack. It then resolves and begins another subgame.

The sequence is not nested unless a Shahrazad is cast inside one of those subgames; it is consecutive. The players complete one entire additional game, return, and immediately begin another because the original spell has not yet resolved.

XII · The Decision

Concession becomes a strategic option

A player may concede a subgame under normal Magic rules. Doing so loses the subgame, returns the players to the main game, and causes the conceding player to lose half their main-game life.

That can be rational. If the main-game position is favorable and the subgame appears likely to consume substantial time or resources, accepting the life loss preserves the chance to win the game that counts toward the match.

Shahrazad therefore presents an unusual offer: play another game for the right to preserve half your life, or pay immediately and continue the original one.

XIII · The Clock

Why Storytime treats it as burn

Old School Storytime decks combine four Shahrazads with Lightning Bolt, Chain Lightning, Psionic Blast, Savannah Lions, Serendib Efreet, and restricted power.

The deck is designed to win the subgame quickly because the remaining library contains the same concentration of cheap creatures and direct damage. Returning to the main game at half life places the opponent within reach of the burn already waiting outside the story.

The card also creates tempo pressure. An opponent who concedes the subgame has effectively allowed a two-mana sorcery to remove half their life. An opponent who plays it out must defeat a deck built specifically to race.

XIV · The Champion

Back-to-back Player’s Ball victories

Aaron West piloted UWR Storytime to the top of the Old School Player’s Ball in 2018 and again in 2019. The latter event contained 103 decks and eight fifty-minute rounds.

The results demonstrate that Shahrazad can be more than a ceremonial oddity under a ruleset that permits four copies. A focused burn shell can use the subgame as a repeatable life-total weapon while still finishing matches within an event’s structure.

The victory is also inseparable from community format policy. The same card that sanctioned Magic bans for tournament logistics became the namesake of a successful Old School deck because the organizer deliberately chose a different social contract.

XV · The Punishment

Underworld Dreams in both worlds

Underworld Dreams punishes every card the opponent draws while it remains on the battlefield. A copy in the main game does not function inside the subgame because effects do not cross between games.

A second copy drawn and cast inside the subgame, however, punishes that game’s draws normally. Black-white Shahrazad decks use four Dreams because the card remains relevant in whichever game contains it.

The strategy attacks the same behavior Shahrazad requires: drawing a new opening hand and beginning another sequence of draw steps. The story offers another chance to play, and Dreams charges life for every new page.

XVI · The Round

The tournament-clock problem

A sanctioned match allocates a finite round time to complete several games. Shahrazad can approximately double the amount of Magic required for one resolution, and additional copies can multiply that demand.

The main-game board must be preserved accurately. Players must shuffle, mulligan, play the subgame, gather every subgame card, restore the outer game, and continue. Nested or consecutive Shahrazads repeat the process.

The problem is not only that games become long. The card can create incentives to consume time after winning the first game of a match, making intentional delay difficult to distinguish from the effect’s ordinary operation.

XVII · The List

Banned, unbanned, and banned again

Shahrazad appeared on Magic’s original banned list alongside the ante cards. It was not prohibited because it dominated through win percentage. It was prohibited because one card could require tournament players to play whole additional games.

The card was unbanned in the Type I and Type 1.5 era in 1999, briefly allowing the subgame experiment back into the oldest sanctioned formats.

Wizards banned it in Legacy and Vintage in September 2007. Current official lists continue to ban it in both formats; Commander and Oathbreaker ban it as well.

XVIII · The Community

Old School keeps telling the story

Old School 93/94 is governed by several independent rulesets. Some ban Shahrazad, some restrict it to one copy, and Eternal Central’s recommended rules have permitted four.

Events that allow the card often establish procedures for preserving the main game, arranging card piles, recording tapped status and counters, and handling matches that approach time.

The result is one of Old School’s clearest expressions of local identity. Choosing a Shahrazad policy is not merely choosing a banned list. It is choosing whether an event wants the full strangeness of 1993 to remain part of competitive play.

XIX · The Favorite

Richard Garfield’s favorite design

Mark Rosewater has repeatedly reported that Richard Garfield identifies Shahrazad as his favorite personal Magic design.

The choice makes sense as game design rather than tournament engineering. The effect is mechanically singular, immediately understandable at a conceptual level, and almost perfectly matched to its source character.

It also represents an early belief that a trading-card game could ask almost anything. The boundary between card effect and complete game structure had not yet become fixed.

XX · The Descendants

The subgame lineage moves to Un-sets

R&D eventually established that black-bordered sets would not create new subgames. The idea survived in Magic’s comedic products.

The under-the-table descendant

Enter the Dungeon

Enter the Dungeon sends players beneath the table to play a subgame whose winner tutors two cards into the main-game hand. The joke works because Shahrazad already taught players what an unnecessary second game looks like.

Once More with Feeling restarted the game rather than nesting one. The Countdown Is at One created a subgame using the players’ libraries and rewarded its winner in the outer game. Event-only designs have continued referencing Shahrazad directly.

The concept remains available where inconvenience is part of the entertainment rather than a threat to tournament procedure.

XXI · The Image

Kaja Foglio’s storyteller

Kaja Foglio depicts Shahrazad surrounded by listeners in an intimate interior. No monster, spell, or visible magical effect appears. The card’s power resides in attention.

The composition directs every face toward the storyteller while her own posture remains controlled. She is not trapped by the audience; the audience is trapped by the need to hear what comes next.

The illustration has never been replaced. Arabian Nights remains the card’s sole paper printing, and the Magic Online version preserves the same art and original-style frame.

XXII · The Habitats

A life outside sanctioned formats

Legacy and Vintage

Explicitly banned

The oldest sanctioned formats exclude the card despite otherwise supporting nearly the entire history of Magic.

Commander and Oathbreaker

Banned from multiplayer construction

A separate game involving several players would multiply both the duration and the difficulty of preserving the main game.

Old School and casual play

Ruleset dependent

Independent communities may ban, restrict, or fully permit Shahrazad and design event procedures around that choice.

The card is also outside Standard, Pioneer, Modern, Pauper, Historic, Timeless, and Brawl by card pool or explicit legality. Its active life belongs to house rules and historical communities.

XXIII · The Feeling

What players remember

The spell resolved, so the game stopped—and another game began in the space where the library had been.
Shahrazad made a single card feel larger than the match containing it.

Players remember writing down the outer battlefield, making a second graveyard, and discovering another Shahrazad several layers below the first.

They remember the opponent asking whether they truly intended to play the subgame or would accept losing half their life. Most of all, they remember returning to the original board and realizing that the story they had just completed had been only one sorcery resolving.

XXV · The Object

Printings and sources

Shahrazad has only one paper printing: Arabian Nights. It is a Reserved List card and has never received a physical reprint, alternate artwork, foil, promotional treatment, or commemorative facsimile. Magic Online added a digital-only version through Masters Edition IV in 2011 using Kaja Foglio’s original artwork.
